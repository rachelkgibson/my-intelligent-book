    Final Project Report

    Objectives

    • Write a complete engineering technical report documenting your design process, prototype, and test results.
    • Apply technical writing conventions: precision, concision, and evidence-first structure.
    • Synthesize your weekly work logs into a polished, professional document.

    Introduction

    A technical report is the primary written artifact of engineering practice. It is how engineers communicate their work to clients, regulators, future team members, and the public record. Writing a good one requires the same precision and discipline as the engineering work itself.

    Your Weekly Work Log documented your project week by week. The Final Project Report synthesizes that record into a coherent, audience-aware document organized around what you did, what you found, and what it means.

    Report Structure

    A technical report is a formal document that communicates the purpose, methods, results, and conclusions of a technical project to a defined audience. It is structured so a reader can navigate to the section they need — not necessarily read front to back.

    A standard engineering technical report follows this structure:

    Section Purpose
    Title Page Project title, author name, date, and course
    Executive Summary / Abstract A 150–250 word summary of the entire report; written last
    Table of Contents Navigational map to all sections
    Introduction Background context, problem statement, and report scope
    Design and Methods What you designed, how you built it, what tools and materials you used
    Results What happened when you tested it; data, observations, models
    Discussion What the results mean; what worked, what did not, and why
    Conclusion Summary of findings and recommendations for future work
    References Properly cited sources using a standard citation style
    Appendices Raw data, detailed calculations, additional figures

    The executive summary is the most widely read section — many stakeholders read only that. Write it so it stands alone: someone who reads only the executive summary should know what problem you solved, what approach you took, and what your key findings were.

    Technical Report Writing

    Technical report writing is the skill of communicating complex engineering information clearly, accurately, and efficiently — without oversimplifying or padding with unnecessary words.

    Three principles govern good technical writing:

    1. Precision — Use exact numbers, units, and terminology. Do not write "the bridge held a lot of weight." Write "the bridge supported 45.3 lbs across a 30 cm span without deflection exceeding 2 mm."
    2. Concision — Eliminate words that do not add meaning. "In order to" → "To." "Due to the fact that" → "Because." Technical readers value their time.
    3. Evidence first — Lead with your finding, then explain how you know. "The structure failed at 38 lbs (12% below the target load of 43 lbs), indicating that joint reinforcement is required" is stronger than a paragraph building to that conclusion.

    Common Student Mistakes

    • Writing in first person ("I built…") when third person is conventional ("The prototype was constructed…")
    • Describing what you planned to do instead of what you actually did
    • Omitting units from all numerical values
    • Placing figures in the report with no preceding sentence that introduces them
    • Writing the executive summary first (write it last, after all other sections are complete)

    Writing from Your Work Log

    Your Weekly Work Log entries are the raw material for this report. Here is how log content maps to report sections:

    Work Log Content Report Section
    What you designed and how you built it Design and Methods
    Test procedures and raw data Results
    Why things worked or did not, what you changed Discussion
    Final state of the design and lessons learned Conclusion

    The report does not reproduce the log — it synthesizes it. Where your log has multiple entries about the same component, the report distills those into a clean, chronological narrative.

    Go to Your Google Doc

    Open your Final Project Report document. Complete all sections in order, but write the Executive Summary last. Use the self-assessment checklist above before submitting. Submit through Canvas by the due date.

    Assignment Summary

    A complete Final Project Report includes:

    • Title page with all required information
    • Executive summary written last, standing alone in 150–250 words
    • Introduction grounded in your original problem statement from Assignment 7
    • Design and methods section describing what you built and how
    • Results section presenting test data with units and proper figure references
    • Discussion interpreting what the results mean honestly — including what did not work
    • Conclusion with at least one specific recommendation for future work
    • References for all sources, in consistent citation format
    • Appendices with raw data tables, calculations, and additional photos

    This report, along with your Final Project Presentation, is the capstone artifact of the course. It represents everything you learned — not just about engineering, but about how to document and communicate technical work professionally.
