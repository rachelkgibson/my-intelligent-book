    Chapter 9: Project Execution, Checkpoints, and Final Presentation

    Summary

    This chapter covers the full project execution phase — from building and testing a prototype, through modeling and simulation checkpoints, to documenting progress in a technical report and delivering a final presentation to stakeholders. Students apply systems thinking to their completed solution and demonstrate the technical and professional readiness they have developed across the entire course.

    Introduction

    You have identified a problem, assessed risks, built a schedule and budget, and pitched your proposal. Now it is time to build, test, document, and present your work. Chapter 9 is where everything comes together.

    This final chapter of the course is different from the ones before it. Instead of learning new frameworks, you are applying every framework you have already learned — the engineering design process, systems thinking, project management, ethics, communication — to your actual practicum project. The goal is not to produce a perfect solution. The goal is to demonstrate that you can work through a real engineering challenge with professionalism, rigor, and the ability to learn from what does not go as planned.

    By the end of this chapter and its assignments, you will have a functioning (or well-documented) prototype, a technical report, and a polished final presentation that you can include in your portfolio.

    Prototype Development

    A prototype is an early physical or digital model of a design that is built to test whether the proposed solution actually works. Prototypes are not finished products — they are learning tools. Their purpose is to reveal problems with your design early, when changes are still relatively cheap and easy to make.

    There are several levels of prototype fidelity, ranging from rough sketches to fully functional models:

    • Low-fidelity prototype — A sketch, cardboard model, or wireframe that tests the basic concept without working components. Fast and cheap to build; best for early-stage testing of shape and form.
    • Mid-fidelity prototype — A partially functional model that tests one or two key mechanisms. Materials may substitute for final materials. Useful for testing specific engineering decisions.
    • High-fidelity prototype — A fully functional model built from final (or near-final) materials that closely resembles the end product. Used for final testing and stakeholder demonstration.

    For your practicum, you may not reach a high-fidelity prototype depending on your project scope and timeline. What matters is that you can demonstrate your design decisions and describe what the final solution would look like and how it would perform.

    Before you build anything, review your project plan from Chapters 7 and 8. Your build schedule should already identify what you will construct, in what order, and by which deadline. If the plan has changed — and it often does — update your work log to reflect the change and explain why.

    Testing and Iteration

    Building a prototype is only the first half of the process. Testing is the systematic process of evaluating how well a prototype meets the design criteria and constraints you defined in Chapter 5. Without testing, you cannot know whether your solution actually solves the problem.

    A structured test plan includes four elements:

    1. What you are testing — One specific criterion or function at a time (e.g., "Does the support structure hold 10 lbs without deflecting more than 0.5 inches?")
    2. How you will test it — The procedure, materials, and tools used
    3. What a passing result looks like — The quantitative or qualitative threshold
    4. What you will record — Data format, units, and number of trials

    After each test, you enter the iteration phase. Iteration means making a targeted change to the design based on what the test revealed, then testing again. This is not failure — it is the engineering design process working exactly as it should. Professional engineers go through dozens of design-test-revise cycles before releasing a product.

    Design Optimization

    Once your prototype passes basic testing — meaning it meets the minimum criteria — you enter design optimization. Optimization is the process of improving the design beyond the minimum threshold: making it lighter, cheaper, stronger, more energy-efficient, or better on any dimension that matters to your stakeholders.

    In engineering, optimization almost always involves trade-offs. Making a structure stronger often makes it heavier. Making a device cheaper often reduces its durability. Your job is not to optimize every dimension at once — that is usually impossible — but to identify which dimensions matter most to your specific users and stakeholders, then improve those while keeping everything else within acceptable bounds.

    Two frameworks that help guide optimization decisions are:

    • Design matrix (Pugh matrix) — A table that scores each design alternative against a set of criteria using +/–/S (better/worse/same as a reference design). Useful for comparing multiple design options systematically.
    • Constraint vs. objective distinction — A constraint is a requirement that must be met (a pass/fail condition); an objective is something to be maximized or minimized. Once constraints are satisfied, optimization focuses on the objectives.

    Document every optimization decision in your work log. Note what you changed, why you changed it, what the trade-off was, and how the test results changed.

    Modeling Techniques

    Before, during, or sometimes instead of physical prototyping, engineers use modeling to represent, analyze, and communicate a design. A model is a simplified representation of a system that captures the aspects most relevant to the question being asked. All models leave some things out — a good model leaves out the right things.

    The most common modeling techniques used in high school and early college engineering include:

    • Sketches and technical drawings — 2D or 3D hand-drawn or CAD representations that communicate geometry, dimensions, and relationships between components
    • Scale models — Physical models built to a fixed ratio of the full-size design; useful for spatial understanding and stakeholder communication
    • Mathematical models — Equations and calculations that describe how the system behaves (e.g., a structural load calculation, a heat transfer equation)
    • Flowcharts and system diagrams — Visual maps of how a process or system works; especially useful for software, processes, and feedback systems
    • Computer-aided design (CAD) models — 3D digital models created in software like Fusion 360, SolidWorks, or Tinkercad that can be viewed from any angle and used to generate manufacturing drawings

    For your practicum, you are expected to include at least one formal model in your Checkpoint 1 submission. The model should represent your current design well enough that a person unfamiliar with your project could understand what you are building and how the key components relate to each other.

    Simulation Software

    Simulation software takes a model one step further by using computation to predict how a system will behave under different conditions — without having to build or test a physical version. Simulations save time and money, allow engineers to test conditions that would be dangerous or impossible in the lab, and help identify failure modes before they happen.

    Several simulation tools are accessible at the high school level:

    Tool Type Common Uses
    MATLAB/Simulink Mathematical / dynamic systems Control systems, signal processing, differential equations
    Fusion 360 (CAM/FEA) CAD + Finite Element Analysis Structural stress, heat distribution
    Tinkercad Circuits Electronics simulation Circuit behavior, Arduino code testing
    GeoGebra Mathematical visualization Geometry, graphing, parametric models
    Excel/Google Sheets Spreadsheet modeling Budget models, data analysis, sensitivity analysis
    PhET Simulations Physics phenomena Forces, energy, waves (free, browser-based)

    You do not need professional-grade simulation software to satisfy Checkpoint 1. A well-structured spreadsheet model or a circuit simulation in Tinkercad may be entirely appropriate for your project. What matters is that your model is accurate, clearly documented, and supports a specific design decision.

    Documentation Standards

    Throughout your project execution, you are expected to maintain accurate, timely, and professional documentation. Documentation standards are the conventions that define what information must be recorded, in what format, with what level of detail, and how it should be organized.

    In professional engineering, documentation is not optional — it is a legal and ethical obligation. Engineers who cannot produce records of their design decisions, test results, and communications may be held liable when something goes wrong. Your work log is the student equivalent of an engineering notebook, and it should reflect the same standards.

    The key principles of good engineering documentation are:

    • Contemporaneous — Record observations at the time they occur, not reconstructed after the fact
    • Complete — Include enough detail that someone else could reproduce your test or understand your decision
    • Accurate — Never alter or falsify records; if you made an error, note it and correct it with a dated entry
    • Organized — Use consistent headings, date stamps, and references to prior entries
    • Objective — Describe what you observed, not just what you expected or wanted to observe

    Your weekly work log should include, at minimum: the date, what you worked on, what you accomplished, what problems arose, and what you plan to do next. Entries written at the end of the week from memory are less valuable than entries written daily as work happens.

    Report Structure

    Before you can write a good technical report, you need to understand what a technical report is and how it is organized. A technical report is a formal document that communicates the purpose, methods, results, and conclusions of a technical project to a defined audience. It differs from a creative writing essay or a research paper in that it is structured, precise, and designed to be navigated — not necessarily read front to back.

    A standard engineering technical report follows this structure:

    Section Purpose
    Title Page Project title, author name, date, and course
    Executive Summary / Abstract A 150–250 word summary of the entire report; written last
    Table of Contents Navigational map to all sections
    Introduction Background context, problem statement, and report scope
    Design and Methods What you designed, how you built it, what tools and materials you used
    Results What happened when you tested it; data, observations, models
    Discussion What the results mean; what worked, what did not, and why
    Conclusion Summary of findings and recommendations for future work
    References Properly cited sources using a standard citation style
    Appendices Raw data, detailed calculations, additional figures

    The executive summary is the most widely read section — many stakeholders read only that. Write it so it stands alone: someone who reads only the executive summary should know what problem you solved, what approach you took, and what your key findings were.

    Technical Report Writing

    With the structure in mind, here is guidance on writing each section effectively at a professional level. Technical report writing is the skill of communicating complex engineering information clearly, accurately, and efficiently — without oversimplifying or padding with unnecessary words.

    Three principles govern good technical writing:

    1. Precision — Use exact numbers, units, and terminology. Do not write "the bridge held a lot of weight." Write "the bridge supported 45.3 lbs across a 30 cm span without deflection exceeding 2 mm."
    2. Concision — Eliminate words that do not add meaning. "In order to" → "To." "Due to the fact that" → "Because." Technical readers value their time.
    3. Evidence first — Lead with your finding, then explain how you know. "The structure failed at 38 lbs (12% below the target load of 43 lbs), indicating that joint reinforcement is required" is stronger than a paragraph building to that conclusion.

    Common student mistakes in technical reports include:

    • Writing in first person ("I built…") when third person or passive voice is conventional ("The prototype was constructed…")
    • Describing what you planned to do instead of what you actually did
    • Omitting units from all numerical values
    • Placing figures in the report with no preceding sentence that introduces them
    • Writing the executive summary first (write it last, after all other sections are complete)

    Stakeholder Identification

    No engineering project exists in a vacuum. Every design affects people — some who will use it, some who will pay for it, some who live near it, some who maintain it. Stakeholders are all individuals, groups, or organizations that have an interest in the outcome of your project, whether or not they are directly involved in building it.

    Stakeholder identification is the process of systematically naming who those people and groups are before finalizing your design and before your final presentation. Identifying stakeholders early allows you to:

    • Tailor your design decisions to real user needs
    • Anticipate objections or concerns before they become problems
    • Invite the right people to your final presentation
    • Address the right audience in your technical report's introduction

    Stakeholder categories commonly relevant to high school practicum projects include:

    • Primary users — The people who will directly use or interact with the solution
    • Decision makers — The people who approve or fund the project (teachers, administrators, family members, community contacts)
    • Affected parties — People who will be indirectly impacted (classmates, community members, future users)
    • Technical reviewers — People with expertise who can evaluate whether the solution is sound (engineers, subject-matter experts, peer reviewers)

    For each stakeholder, record their name or role, their interest in your project, and how you plan to communicate with them.

    Stakeholder Communication

    Identifying stakeholders is only the first step. Stakeholder communication is the ongoing process of keeping the right people informed at the right times, in the right format. Effective communication with stakeholders throughout a project prevents surprises at the final presentation and builds the kind of trust that turns a class project into a genuine community contribution.

    There are three categories of stakeholder communication in a practicum project:

    • Progress updates — Brief, scheduled check-ins that tell stakeholders where the project stands relative to the plan. Your Checkpoint 2 submission is a formal version of this.
    • Feedback requests — Structured conversations or surveys that invite stakeholders to evaluate a prototype or a design direction. Design feedback early in execution is more valuable than feedback at the final presentation.
    • Final presentation — A formal, prepared communication that demonstrates the completed (or current-best) solution, explains the design process, presents evidence, and invites questions.

    The tone and depth of communication should match the audience. A technical stakeholder (an engineer or a science teacher) can handle design specifications and test data. A non-technical stakeholder (a community member, a parent) needs the same information framed in terms of impact and plain language.

    Final Project Presentation

    The final project presentation is the culminating public demonstration of your practicum work. It is your opportunity to communicate — clearly, confidently, and professionally — what problem you solved, how you solved it, what you learned in the process, and what you would do differently given more time or resources.

    A strong final presentation has four qualities:

    1. Clarity — The audience understands what the project is, why it matters, and what you did, without needing to ask for clarification.
    2. Evidence — Claims are backed by data, test results, or documented observations — not just assertions.
    3. Narrative — The presentation tells a coherent story: problem → design process → solution → results → reflection. It does not just list what you did.
    4. Professionalism — Dress, delivery, slide design, and demeanor match the norms of a professional engineering review.

    A well-structured final presentation typically follows this sequence:

    • Hook (30 seconds) — Open with the problem in human terms: who is affected, how significantly, and why the current situation is unsatisfactory
    • Problem statement (1–2 minutes) — Define the challenge, its scope, and your design criteria
    • Design process (2–3 minutes) — Walk through key decisions, showing your prototype or model and explaining the iteration process
    • Results (2–3 minutes) — Present test data and evidence of performance against your criteria
    • Reflection (1–2 minutes) — What worked, what did not, and what you would do next
    • Q&A (3–5 minutes) — Prepared responses to likely questions; know your data

    Professional Readiness

    Completing your practicum project is not just an academic achievement — it is evidence of professional readiness, the combination of technical skills, communication abilities, work habits, and personal qualities that employers and postsecondary programs look for in early-career engineers.

    Professional readiness is demonstrated through actions, not claims. The behaviors you have practiced throughout this course — meeting deadlines, documenting your work, incorporating feedback, communicating with stakeholders, and recovering from setbacks — are exactly the behaviors that distinguish a prepared professional from someone who merely knows the theory.

    As you finish the course, reflect on the following dimensions of professional readiness and consider how your practicum experience provides evidence for each:

    • Technical competence — Can you apply engineering tools, methods, and principles to a real problem?
    • Project management — Did you stay on schedule and within budget? How did you recover when you did not?
    • Communication — Can you explain your work clearly in writing (the technical report) and in person (the final presentation)?
    • Ethics and integrity — Did you document your work honestly, cite your sources, and make design decisions that prioritized safety?
    • Adaptability — How did you respond when a test failed, a material was unavailable, or your initial design did not work?

    These are also the themes your portfolio should highlight. When you update your portfolio after this course — building on the work you began in Chapter 3 — include your final technical report, a photograph or video of your prototype, a slide from your presentation, and a brief written reflection on what you learned.

    • Design Optimization — Move beyond minimum viability by improving performance on the dimensions your stakeholders value most.
    • Modeling Techniques — Represent your design in a format others can understand; match the model type to the question you are answering.
    • Simulation Software — Use accessible tools (CAD, spreadsheets, circuit simulators) to predict behavior before committing to a build.
    • Documentation Standards — Record work contemporaneously, completely, and honestly; your work log is a professional document.
    • Report Structure — Organize your technical report around standard engineering sections; write the executive summary last.
    • Technical Report Writing — Lead with findings, use precise units and terminology, and let evidence carry the argument.
    • Stakeholder Identification — Know who is affected by your project before you finalize your design or your presentation invitation list.
    • Stakeholder Communication — Communicate progress and invite feedback throughout execution, not only at the end.
    • Final Project Presentation — Tell a coherent story with a hook, evidence, and honest reflection; prepare for Q&A.
    • Professional Readiness — The behaviors you practiced across this course are the evidence of readiness that matters in engineering.

    Your final presentation and technical report are the last two artifacts you will add to your portfolio this year. Make them something you are proud to show.
