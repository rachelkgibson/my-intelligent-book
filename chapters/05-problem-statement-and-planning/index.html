    Chapter 5: Problem Statement, Planning, Ethics, and IP

    Summary

    This is the launch chapter for the practicum project. Students identify a real engineering need or opportunity, define their challenge, and begin applying project management thinking to their solution. Woven throughout is an introduction to the ethical and legal responsibilities engineers carry — including professional ethics, intellectual property rights, and the obligations engineers have to the public — framed as essential context for making good design decisions.

    Introduction

    Every engineering project begins not with a solution, but with a problem. Before you can design anything, you need to clearly understand what challenge you are trying to address, who is affected by it, and what limitations your solution must work within. This chapter walks you through that process — from noticing a problem in the world around you to writing a focused engineering challenge definition that will anchor your practicum project.

    But defining a good problem is only part of what engineers do at the start of a project. Engineers also carry significant responsibilities: to their clients, to the public, and to the profession itself. This chapter introduces the ethical codes that govern engineering practice and the intellectual property laws that protect creative and technical work. Understanding both sets of responsibilities will help you make better decisions as you design and document your project.

    Part 1: Defining the Problem

    Problem Identification

    Problem identification is the process of recognizing that a gap exists between how things currently work and how they could or should work. In engineering, a problem is not simply an annoyance — it is an observable condition that causes harm, inefficiency, or unmet need for a specific group of people or a system.

    Engineers find problems by observing the world carefully, listening to the people who experience a gap, and asking questions like:

    • Who is affected, and how often?
    • What is the cost — in time, money, safety, or quality of life — of leaving this problem unsolved?
    • Has someone already tried to solve it? Why did that approach fall short?

    Not every frustration is a viable engineering problem. A problem worth engineering has a measurable impact, a definable scope, and at least the possibility of a designed solution.

    Needs Assessment

    Once you have identified a candidate problem, the next step is a needs assessment — a structured investigation that helps you confirm the problem is real, understand it fully, and determine whether it is worth solving. A needs assessment typically involves four activities:

    1. Stakeholder interviews — talking directly to people who experience the problem
    2. Data collection — gathering numbers that quantify the impact (how many people, how often, what cost)
    3. Root cause analysis — asking "why does this problem exist?" repeatedly until you reach the underlying cause rather than a surface symptom
    4. Literature review — researching whether existing solutions exist and why they may be insufficient

    The output of a needs assessment is a clear statement of the gap: what is happening now, what should be happening, and what is preventing the improvement.

    Opportunity Recognition

    Problems and opportunities are two sides of the same coin. Opportunity recognition is the skill of reframing a problem as a specific, actionable opportunity for a designed solution. This shift in framing matters because it moves your thinking from what is wrong to what could be built.

    Compare these two framings of the same situation:

    Problem Frame Opportunity Frame
    "Students at our school waste time looking for open parking spaces." "An opportunity exists to design a low-cost sensor system that displays real-time parking availability on a map."
    "Elderly residents in this neighborhood can't read food labels easily." "An opportunity exists to develop a smartphone app that reads and enlarges nutrition information using the phone's camera."
    "The school's garden irrigation wastes water during rainy weeks." "An opportunity exists to build an automated irrigation controller that checks a weather API before opening the valves."

    Notice that the opportunity frame names a specific intervention and a specific beneficiary. This specificity is what makes the opportunity actionable.

    Engineering Challenge Definition

    An engineering challenge definition (sometimes called a problem statement) is a concise, written statement that captures the problem, the stakeholders affected, the measurable impact, and the constraints and goals for a potential solution. It is the single most important document you will write at the start of your project, because every design decision you make later should trace back to it.

    A well-written engineering challenge definition has four components:

    1. The situation — a one-sentence description of the current state and who is affected
    2. The need — a clear statement of what is missing or insufficient
    3. The impact — a quantified description of the cost of the problem
    4. The goal — what a successful solution would accomplish, expressed in measurable terms

    Example:

    Students at [School Name] spend an average of 8 minutes per day searching for open parking spots during peak arrival hours, resulting in tardiness for approximately 30 students per week. A successful solution would reduce average search time to under 2 minutes without requiring students to download an app or access the school's Wi-Fi network.

    This statement is specific, measurable, and does not prescribe a particular solution — it leaves the design space open.

    Constraints and Criteria

    Before brainstorming solutions, engineers define the boundaries within which any solution must operate. These boundaries take two forms: constraints and criteria.

    A constraint is a non-negotiable limit — a condition that any valid solution must satisfy. Constraints come from physics, safety regulations, available materials, time, budget, or the needs of stakeholders. If a solution violates a constraint, it is not a viable solution regardless of its other merits.

    A criterion (plural: criteria) is a desired quality — a goal the solution should achieve as much as possible. Unlike constraints, criteria can be partially satisfied. They are used to compare solutions and choose between alternatives.

    Type Definition Example
    Constraint Must be satisfied (binary — yes or no) Solution must cost less than $200 to build
    Constraint Must be satisfied Solution must not require a WiFi connection
    Criterion Should be maximized or optimized Solution should be as lightweight as possible
    Criterion Should be maximized or optimized Solution should be usable by people of all ages

    Listing your constraints and criteria before you brainstorm keeps your design process honest — it prevents you from falling in love with an idea that cannot actually work.

    Brainstorming Solutions

    Brainstorming is a structured ideation technique for generating a large number of candidate solutions before evaluating any of them. The critical rule of productive brainstorming is deferred judgment: during the generative phase, no idea is criticized or eliminated. Premature evaluation kills creative thinking.

    Effective brainstorming techniques for engineering include:

    • Mind mapping — starting with the engineering challenge at the center and branching outward into different solution categories
    • SCAMPER — systematically asking whether existing solutions can be Substituted, Combined, Adapted, Modified, Put to other uses, Eliminated, or Reversed
    • Crazy eights — sketching eight rough solution concepts in eight minutes to push past obvious first ideas
    • Biomimicry prompting — asking "how does nature solve a similar problem?"

    After the generative phase, you evaluate candidates against your constraints and criteria — eliminating those that violate constraints and ranking the rest by how well they satisfy the criteria. The goal is to narrow to two or three strong candidates worth developing further.

    Project Management

    Project management is the discipline of planning, organizing, and controlling the work required to complete a defined goal within a given time and budget. For your practicum, project management is not optional — without it, well-intentioned projects stall, run over budget, and miss deadlines.

    The five core functions of project management are:

    1. Scope definition — specifying exactly what the project will and will not include
    2. Scheduling — breaking the work into tasks and assigning start and end dates
    3. Resource planning — identifying what materials, tools, and time are needed
    4. Risk management — anticipating what could go wrong and planning responses
    5. Communication — keeping stakeholders informed of progress, changes, and decisions

    You will build detailed project management artifacts — a work breakdown structure, a Gantt chart, and a budget — in Chapters 6 and 7. Here, the goal is to understand project management as a mindset: engineering success is not just about a good idea; it is about executing that idea systematically.

    Part 2: Engineering Ethics

    Engineering Ethics and the Code of Ethics

    Engineering ethics is the branch of applied ethics that examines the moral principles and professional values that should guide engineers in their work. Because engineers design systems and structures that affect public safety, the environment, and the quality of daily life, the ethical stakes of engineering decisions are unusually high.

    Most major engineering professional societies publish a formal Code of Ethics — a written set of principles that members are expected to uphold. While the specific language varies by organization (NSPE, ASCE, IEEE, ASME all publish their own), every engineering code of ethics shares a common core:

    • Hold the safety, health, and welfare of the public as the paramount concern
    • Be honest and impartial in professional reports, statements, and testimony
    • Act with competence — only undertake work within your area of expertise
    • Avoid conflicts of interest or disclose them fully when they cannot be avoided
    • Protect confidential information entrusted by clients or employers

    These codes are not merely aspirational. Licensed Professional Engineers who violate them can face suspension or revocation of their license by the state engineering board.

    Professional Responsibility and Public Safety

    Professional responsibility refers to an engineer's obligation to perform their work competently, honestly, and with regard for the consequences of their designs on others. This responsibility is especially weighty because engineers often design systems that members of the public use without any ability to evaluate their safety independently.

    Public safety in engineering is the principle that no commercial, competitive, or personal interest justifies knowingly placing the public at risk. Engineering history contains sobering examples of what happens when this principle is violated or ignored:

    • The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (1940) — resonance effects that engineers understood theoretically were not adequately tested at scale.
    • The Challenger Space Shuttle disaster (1986) — engineers raised O-ring concerns before launch; organizational pressure contributed to the decision to proceed.
    • The Flint, Michigan water crisis (2014–2019) — cost-cutting decisions led to lead contamination of the public water supply, affecting thousands of children.

    These cases are studied not to assign blame decades later, but because they reveal the conditions under which ethical failures occur — and the systematic approaches that can prevent them.

    Ethical Decision Making

    Ethical decision making in engineering is the process of recognizing an ethical dimension in a situation, identifying the stakeholders and values involved, evaluating available options against ethical principles, and choosing a course of action that can be professionally defended.

    A widely used framework for engineering ethics cases is the following four-step process:

    1. Recognize the ethical issue — Is there a potential for harm? Whose interests are in conflict?
    2. Gather the facts — What do you know? What do you need to find out? Who has relevant expertise?
    3. Evaluate the options — Which option best upholds the core engineering values? What are the consequences of each option for each stakeholder?
    4. Act and reflect — Choose the most defensible option, document your reasoning, and reflect on the outcome.

    Personal vs. Professional Ethics

    A distinction that surprises many students is that personal ethics and professional ethics are not always the same. Personal ethics refers to the values and moral principles a person holds as an individual — shaped by family, culture, religion, and experience. Professional ethics refers to the standards of conduct defined by the profession itself, independent of any individual's personal beliefs.

    This distinction matters in engineering because you may encounter situations where your personal preferences differ from what your professional code requires:

    • Personal belief: "I trust my client's word." Professional obligation: An engineer must verify safety-critical claims independently.
    • Personal preference: "I don't want to make trouble by raising this concern." Professional obligation: If you observe a safety hazard, you are obligated to report it.

    The professional code is not a replacement for personal ethics — it is a floor, a minimum standard that all engineers must meet regardless of their individual values. Most engineers find that their personal and professional ethics align closely, but when they conflict, the professional code governs professional conduct.

    Conflict of Interest

    A conflict of interest exists when an engineer's personal, financial, or other interests could compromise — or appear to compromise — their professional judgment. Conflicts of interest are among the most common ethical challenges in engineering practice.

    Examples of conflicts of interest in engineering:

    • An engineer who owns stock in a materials supplier recommends that supplier's products on a client project without disclosing the ownership.
    • An engineer reviewing a competitor's design for a government contract has a personal friendship with the competitor's lead engineer.
    • An engineer accepts a gift from a vendor who wants to be included in an upcoming project specification.

    The standard response to a conflict of interest is disclosure — informing all affected parties and, if the conflict cannot be adequately managed, recusing yourself from the decision. The NSPE Code of Ethics states that engineers shall "act in such a manner as to uphold and enhance the honor, integrity, and dignity of the engineering profession" — which requires proactively managing conflicts even when disclosure is uncomfortable.

    Part 3: Intellectual Property

    What Is Intellectual Property?

    Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind — inventions, designs, artistic works, symbols, names, and trade information — that are protected by law. The purpose of IP law is to encourage innovation by giving creators exclusive rights to benefit from their work for a defined period, while ultimately releasing the work into the public domain for everyone's benefit.

    For engineers, IP is not an abstract legal concept — it is a practical reality that shapes what you can design, what you can document publicly, and what protections you can seek for your own innovations. Understanding the four main categories of IP protection is essential before you begin designing and documenting your practicum project.

    Patents

    A patent is a legal right granted by the federal government (via the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office, USPTO) that gives the inventor the exclusive right to make, use, and sell an invention for a limited period — typically 20 years from the filing date. In exchange for this exclusive right, the inventor must publicly disclose exactly how the invention works.

    The three types of patents most relevant to engineering are:

    • Utility patents — protect new and useful processes, machines, articles of manufacture, or compositions of matter (the most common type for engineering inventions)
    • Design patents — protect the ornamental or aesthetic design of a functional item (14 years)
    • Plant patents — protect distinct and new plant varieties (less common in engineering)

    For an invention to be patentable, it must be novel (not previously disclosed), non-obvious (not an obvious improvement to someone skilled in the field), and useful. The patent application process is complex and typically requires a registered patent attorney or agent.

    Copyrights

    A copyright is a form of IP protection that automatically applies to original creative works the moment they are created and fixed in a tangible form — no registration required, though registration with the U.S. Copyright Office provides legal advantages. Copyright protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself.

    In engineering contexts, copyright protects:

    • Technical reports, white papers, and written documentation
    • Engineering drawings and schematics
    • Software source code
    • Photographs and illustrations used in project materials

    Copyright lasts for the life of the author plus 70 years (for individual authors). For corporate or "work for hire" authorship, the term is 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation, whichever is shorter.

    Important distinction: Copyright does not protect the underlying engineering concepts or methods described in a document. To protect an invention itself, a patent is required.

    Trademarks

    A trademark is a word, phrase, logo, symbol, or combination thereof that identifies the source of a product or service and distinguishes it from competitors. Trademarks are primarily relevant to commercial engineering products rather than engineering processes.

    Examples of trademarks in engineering-adjacent contexts:

    • The LEGO® name and brick design
    • The Intel® logo and the "Intel Inside" phrase
    • The caterpillar tracks design associated with Caterpillar Inc.

    Trademark rights can last indefinitely as long as the mark remains in use and is actively enforced. Marks registered with the USPTO may use the ® symbol; unregistered marks that have been used in commerce may use the ™ symbol.

    Trade Secrets

    A trade secret is confidential business information that provides a competitive advantage and is protected by keeping it secret — there is no registration process, and protection lasts as long as the information remains confidential. The Coca-Cola formula and Google's search algorithm are famous examples.

    In engineering practice, trade secrets commonly include:

    • Proprietary manufacturing processes
    • Customer lists and pricing strategies
    • Internal engineering specifications not disclosed in patents
    • Software algorithms and source code (when kept confidential rather than patented or copyrighted)

    The legal protection for trade secrets requires that the owner take "reasonable measures" to keep the information secret — such as non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), restricted access to documents, and secure storage of digital files.

    IP Protection and Enforcement

    Obtaining IP protection is one step; enforcing that protection is another. IP protection and enforcement refers to the legal mechanisms and actions available to the holder of an IP right when that right is violated.

    Key enforcement mechanisms include:

    • Civil litigation — filing a lawsuit in federal court seeking an injunction (stop order) and monetary damages
    • Cease and desist letters — a written demand to stop infringing activity, often the first step before litigation
    • International Trade Commission (ITC) proceedings — used to block importation of infringing goods at the U.S. border
    • DMCA takedowns — used to remove infringing digital content under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act

    Enforcement is expensive and time-consuming, which is why large corporations employ legal departments dedicated to IP matters. For individual inventors and small engineering firms, the cost of enforcement can sometimes exceed the value of the protected innovation — a practical reality that influences how engineers and companies decide what to protect and how.

    IP Infringement

    IP infringement occurs when someone makes, uses, sells, or distributes protected IP without permission from the rights holder. The consequences of infringement can include civil damages (often calculated as the infringer's profits or the rights holder's lost profits), attorney's fees, and in cases involving willful infringement or trade secret theft, criminal penalties.

    For student engineers, the most common IP risk areas are:

    • Using patented components or processes in a design without a license
    • Copying software libraries or code without complying with the applicable open-source license
    • Reproducing images, diagrams, or text from published sources without attribution or permission (copyright infringement)

    Fair use is a legal doctrine that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission — for purposes such as education, commentary, criticism, or research. However, fair use is a defense evaluated case-by-case, not a blanket exemption. When in doubt about whether a use is permissible, cite the source, seek permission, or find an openly licensed alternative.

    IP Violation Example Potential Consequence
    Patent infringement Selling a device that uses a patented mechanism without a license Civil lawsuit; injunction; damages
    Copyright infringement Reproducing a textbook diagram in a report without permission DMCA takedown; civil lawsuit
    Trademark infringement Using a competitor's logo on your product Injunction; reputational damage
    Trade secret theft Sharing an employer's proprietary process with a competitor Criminal charges; civil lawsuit

    Chapter Summary

    This chapter covered the foundational work of starting an engineering project responsibly. Here is what you should be able to explain and apply:

    Defining the Problem:

    • Engineers begin with careful problem identification — not every frustration is a viable engineering problem, but those with measurable impact and a definable scope are.
    • A needs assessment confirms the problem is real and helps you understand it at the root cause level.
    • Opportunity recognition reframes problems as specific, actionable design opportunities with named beneficiaries.
    • An engineering challenge definition captures the situation, need, measurable impact, and goal in a single focused statement.
    • Constraints (non-negotiable limits) and criteria (desired qualities) define the design space before brainstorming begins.
    • Brainstorming uses deferred judgment to generate many ideas before evaluating any of them.
    • Project management applies throughout: scope, schedule, resources, risk, and communication are all managed from day one.

    Engineering Ethics:

    • Engineering codes of ethics universally place public safety and welfare as the paramount obligation.
    • Professional responsibility means taking ownership of the safety, accuracy, and consequences of your work.
    • Ethical decision making is a four-step process: recognize, gather facts, evaluate options, act and reflect.
    • Professional ethics are a minimum floor that governs conduct regardless of personal preferences.
    • Conflicts of interest must be disclosed promptly and managed to preserve professional integrity.

    Intellectual Property:

    • Patents protect inventions for 20 years in exchange for public disclosure.
    • Copyrights automatically protect original creative expression from the moment of creation.
    • Trademarks protect brand identifiers that distinguish products in the marketplace.
    • Trade secrets protect confidential information through secrecy rather than registration.
    • IP infringement carries civil and sometimes criminal consequences — understanding what you may and may not use is a professional obligation.

    All of these concepts apply directly to your practicum project. Your problem statement is due as the first deliverable. Make it specific, measurable, and grounded in a real need.
