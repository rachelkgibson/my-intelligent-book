    Weekly Work Log

    Objectives

    • Maintain a weekly engineering work log documenting progress, problems, and decisions.
    • Compare actual progress to your project schedule each week.
    • Build a log that will serve as the evidence base for your Final Project Report.

    Introduction

    The Weekly Work Log runs from the week after your Project Proposal Pitch through the week of your Final Project Presentation — approximately 20 weeks. A log submitted at the end of a week from memory is far less valuable than one written as work happens. Get in the habit of making brief notes during or immediately after every work session.

    Your work log serves two purposes simultaneously: it is a graded assignment submitted weekly, and it is the source material you will draw from when writing your Final Project Report. Students who keep thorough logs write far better final reports.

    Documentation Standards

    Documentation standards are the conventions that define what information must be recorded, in what format, with what level of detail, and how it should be organized. In professional engineering, documentation is not optional — it is a legal and ethical obligation. Engineers who cannot produce records of their design decisions, test results, and communications may be held liable when something goes wrong.

    The key principles of good engineering documentation:

    • Contemporaneous — Record observations at the time they occur, not reconstructed after the fact
    • Complete — Include enough detail that someone else could reproduce your test or understand your decision
    • Accurate — Never alter or falsify records; if you made an error, note it with a dated correction
    • Organized — Use consistent headings, date stamps, and references to prior entries
    • Objective — Describe what you observed, not just what you expected or hoped to observe

    What to Include Each Week

    Every weekly log entry must include:

    Field What to Write
    Date range The week (e.g., "Week of November 4–8")
    What I worked on Specific tasks — not "I worked on the project" but "I soldered the sensor to the breadboard and ran the first connectivity test"
    What I accomplished Concrete outputs — files created, components built, tests run, results recorded
    Problems encountered What went wrong, what did not work, what was harder than expected
    How I responded What you did about the problem — or what you plan to do next week
    Next steps The specific tasks planned for the following week

    Avoid These Log Mistakes

    • Vague entries: "Worked on the project for about an hour" tells a reviewer nothing.
    • Reconstructed logs: Writing three weeks of logs in one sitting is obvious and loses the contemporaneous quality that makes logs useful.
    • Skipping problem weeks: The weeks when things went wrong are the most important to document thoroughly.

    The Log as a Project Management Tool

    Your work log is not just a record — it is a real-time project management tool. Each week, compare your actual progress to the schedule you created in Assignment 12. Are you on track? Behind? Ahead?

    If you are behind, document why and what you are doing to catch up. If your schedule needs to change, update it and note the change in the log with a brief explanation. Reviewers and your teacher are not looking for a project that went perfectly — they are looking for a student who identified problems and responded thoughtfully.

    Submit Each Week

    Your Weekly Work Log is submitted through Canvas by the due date listed in the Canvas module each week. Late logs lose value because they cannot be contemporaneous. Submit on time even if the entry is brief.

    Assignment Summary

    The Weekly Work Log is a continuous assignment, not a one-time submission. By the time your project is complete, your log should:

    • Cover every week from post-pitch through final presentation
    • Document specific tasks, accomplishments, and problems each week
    • Reflect honest comparison to your project schedule
    • Serve as the evidence base for your Final Project Report (Assignment 18)

    Think of it as the engineering notebook your future self will be glad you kept.
