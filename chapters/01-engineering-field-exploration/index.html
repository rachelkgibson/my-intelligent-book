    Engineering Field Exploration Lab

    Objectives

    • Research a field of engineering or STEM that interests you.
    • Identify a possible topic or focus area for your capstone project or a potential internship.

    Part 1: Pick an Engineering Field of Interest

    Skills Assessed

    CTE-39: Research a principal field of interest in engineering and its scope.

    Engineering Fields

    Engineering is not a single career — it is a family of dozens of distinct disciplines, each with its own focus, tools, and professional community. Choosing a field of interest is the process of matching your personal strengths, curiosity, and long-term goals to a specific discipline. This is not a permanent decision, but it matters for how you focus your research in this lab.

    The list below is not exhaustive, but it covers the major fields of engineering. Use it as a starting point to choose one field you want to explore. If you are interested in another STEM field, feel free to research that instead — just let your instructor know.

    • Aerospace
    • Agricultural
    • Architectural
    • Bioengineering / Biomedical
    • Chemical
    • Civil
    • Computer
    • Electrical
    • Environmental
    • Industrial
    • Manufacturing
    • Marine / Ocean
    • Materials
    • Mechanical
    • Mining
    • Naval Architectural / Nautical
    • Nuclear
    • Petroleum
    • Safety
    • Software
    • Systems

    Engineering career research is the systematic process of gathering, evaluating, and organizing information about what professionals in a field actually do, how they are compensated, and what pathway leads there. Good career research uses multiple source types — relying on a single website gives you an incomplete picture.

    Part 2: Educational Requirements

    Skills Assessed

    CTE-40: Identify educational requirements for the chosen engineering field.

    What Does It Take to Get There?

    Every engineering specialization has a defined educational pathway. Degree requirements are the specific courses, credit hours, and cumulative GPA thresholds a student must satisfy to earn an engineering degree. While exact requirements vary by school and discipline, most accredited engineering programs share a common structure:

    • Mathematics and basic sciences — calculus, differential equations, linear algebra, chemistry, and physics
    • Engineering sciences — discipline-specific foundational courses (circuit analysis, thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, etc.)
    • Engineering design — a senior capstone project that mirrors real professional work
    • General education — composition, social sciences, and humanities
    • Technical electives — specialized courses that allow students to focus on a sub-specialty

    ABET accreditation (Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology) is the most important quality signal when evaluating engineering programs. Employers and licensing boards recognize ABET-accredited programs as meeting national standards. Always check for ABET accreditation before committing to a school.

    Part 3: Post-Secondary Education Opportunities

    Skills Assessed

    CTE-41: Research postsecondary education opportunities related to engineering.

    What Are Your Options?

    Postsecondary education refers to any formal learning that takes place after high school. Before choosing a specific school or program, take time to explore the full range of options — the right pathway depends on your goals, your finances, and the field you have chosen.

    Choose three different pathways from the list below to research for your chosen field:

    • Technical / trade schools
    • Community colleges
    • Four-year colleges and universities
    • Military training
    • Advanced degree programs (Master's degree / Ph.D.)
    • Scholarships

    This is an excellent opportunity to identify colleges or training programs you might actually apply to. The college admissions process for engineering programs typically involves standardized test scores, a strong math and science transcript, a personal statement, and letters of recommendation. Engineering majors are often among the most competitive at a given school — researching programs now gives you time to strengthen your application.

    Part 4: Professional Certifications and Licensure

    Skills Assessed

    CTE-42: Compare requirements for select professional engineering certifications and licensure.

    A Deeper Dive

    Professional credentials in engineering come in two forms: certifications and licenses. Understanding the difference — and knowing what each requires — is essential for planning your career.

    A professional certification is a credential awarded by a professional organization that demonstrates competence in a specific area or technology. Certifications are voluntary but valued by employers. Examples include the Project Management Professional (PMP), Certified Energy Manager (CEM), and LEED AP. Requirements vary, but most involve passing an exam and sometimes accumulating work experience.

    Licensure is the legal authorization — issued by a state government — that permits an engineer to offer professional engineering services to the public. The path to licensure has four stages:

    1. ABET-accredited degree — Graduate from an accredited engineering program
    2. FE Exam / EIT — Pass the Fundamentals of Engineering exam to earn the Engineer in Training (EIT) designation
    3. Supervised experience — Work at least four years under a licensed Professional Engineer
    4. PE Exam — Pass the discipline-specific Principles and Practice of Engineering exam to earn the Professional Engineer (PE) License

    The PE License is the highest standard credential in the profession. Once licensed, a PE can sign and seal engineering documents and take legal responsibility for designs.

    Research Questions

    Use the websites from Parts 1 and 2, plus any additional sources, to investigate the following for your chosen field:

    • What baseline credentials or specific certifications are required or commonly expected?
    • What kinds of professional certifications and licenses are available in this field?
    • What are the requirements for obtaining them (education, exam, experience, cost)?
    • How do requirements change if you work outside the United States?
    Credential Type Issuing Authority Legal Requirement? Example
    Professional Certification Professional association or vendor No — voluntary PMP, CEM, LEED AP
    PE License State government licensing board Yes — for public-safety work PE (Civil, Mechanical, etc.)
