    Chapter 1: Engineering Field Exploration

    Summary

    This chapter launches the course by helping students identify and research an engineering field that interests them. Students explore engineering specializations, investigate educational and licensing pathways, and compare certification and degree requirements — building the foundation for every career decision that follows in the course.

    Introduction

    Engineering is not a single career — it is a family of dozens of distinct disciplines, each with its own focus, tools, and professional community. Before you can plan your education or set career goals, you need to understand which branch of engineering fits your interests and strengths. This chapter is your starting point for that exploration.

    By the end of this chapter you will be able to name the major engineering specializations, describe the education and licensure path in your chosen field, and explain what the EIT exam and the PE license are and why they matter. All of this feeds directly into your Engineering Field Exploration Lab, where you will research one specific field in depth and present your findings to the class.

    Engineering Specializations

    An engineering specialization is a branch of engineering that focuses on a particular type of problem, technology, or physical system. Each specialization has a professional community, university programs, and a licensing pathway. The table below introduces the most common specializations you will encounter in your research.

    Specialization Core Focus Example Projects
    Civil Engineering Infrastructure: roads, bridges, water systems Highway design, stormwater management
    Mechanical Engineering Machines, heat transfer, materials Engine design, HVAC systems, robotics
    Electrical Engineering Circuits, power, signals Power grids, microchips, communication systems
    Chemical Engineering Chemical processes, materials at scale Pharmaceuticals, fuel production, polymers
    Computer Engineering Hardware-software integration Embedded systems, computer architecture
    Environmental Engineering Pollution control, sustainability Water treatment, air quality monitoring
    Aerospace Engineering Aircraft and spacecraft Commercial jets, satellites, drones
    Biomedical Engineering Medical devices and systems Prosthetics, imaging equipment, biosensors
    Industrial Engineering Process efficiency and systems Manufacturing flow, supply chain, ergonomics
    Software Engineering Software systems and architecture Mobile apps, operating systems, data pipelines

    This table is a starting point, not an exhaustive list. There are also emerging specializations — such as materials engineering, nuclear engineering, and systems engineering — that cross the boundaries between the disciplines above.

    Field of Interest Selection

    Choosing a field of interest is not a permanent decision, but it is an important one for this course. Your choice shapes the research you do, the careers you explore, and the lens through which you design your practicum project.

    Field of Interest Selection is the process of matching your personal strengths, curiosity, and long-term goals to a specific engineering discipline. Start by asking three questions:

    1. What problems do I find compelling? (Environmental crises, transportation, health care, technology?)
    2. What subjects come most naturally to me? (Biology, physics, computer science, design?)
    3. What does a typical day in this field look like? (Lab work, field work, design work, data analysis?)

    Answering these questions honestly will help you narrow your focus before you begin formal research.

    Engineering Career Research

    Once you have a field of interest in mind, the next step is engineering career research — the systematic process of gathering, evaluating, and organizing information about what professionals in that field actually do, how they are compensated, and what pathway leads there.

    Effective career research uses multiple source types. Relying on a single source — say, one website or one person's experience — gives you an incomplete picture. The sources below each serve a different purpose:

    • Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) Occupational Outlook Handbook — national salary data, employment projections, and education requirements for every engineering occupation
    • Professional society websites (e.g., ASCE, ASME, IEEE, AIChE) — field-specific news, student membership resources, and career guidance from practicing engineers
    • LinkedIn and job postings — real-world qualifications that employers are asking for right now
    • Informational interviews — conversations with engineers working in your target field, which reveal the day-to-day realities that statistics cannot capture
    • College program pages — what courses you would take and what specializations the program offers

    Gathering information is only the first step. Evaluating that information critically — checking publication dates, considering source biases, comparing multiple perspectives — is what makes the research useful.

    Postsecondary Education Options

    Engineering careers almost always require education beyond high school. The term postsecondary education refers to any formal learning that takes place after secondary school (high school). Before choosing a specific program, it helps to understand the full range of options and what each one offers.

    The three main postsecondary pathways that lead to engineering careers are described below.

    Pathway Credential Typical Duration Engineering Access
    Community College + Transfer Associate's Degree, then Bachelor's 2 + 2 years Transfer to 4-year engineering program
    4-Year University Bachelor of Science (B.S.) 4 years Direct entry to engineering jobs and licensure path
    Graduate School Master's (M.S.) or Doctorate (Ph.D.) 2–5 years beyond B.S. Research, specialized roles, academia

    Community college is a cost-effective way to complete general education requirements before transferring to a four-year engineering program. Many states have guaranteed transfer agreements between community colleges and public universities — check Virginia's Community College System (VCCS) for local examples.

    A Bachelor of Science in Engineering (or in a specific discipline like Civil, Electrical, or Mechanical Engineering) is the standard credential for entry-level engineering positions and is required to begin the licensure process. Graduate degrees are optional for many careers but required for advanced research roles and some leadership positions.

    Engineering Programs and Degree Requirements

    An engineering program is a structured curriculum at a college or university that leads to a degree in a specific engineering discipline. Not all programs are equivalent. Accreditation by ABET (Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology) is the most important quality signal — employers and licensing boards recognize ABET-accredited programs as meeting national standards.

    Degree requirements are the specific courses, credit hours, and cumulative GPA thresholds a student must satisfy to earn the degree. While requirements vary by school and discipline, ABET-accredited programs share a common core structure:

    • Mathematics and basic science — typically calculus through differential equations, linear algebra, chemistry, and physics (roughly 30–35% of coursework)
    • Engineering sciences — discipline-specific foundational courses (fluid mechanics, circuit analysis, thermodynamics, etc.)
    • Engineering design — culminating in a senior capstone project that mirrors real professional practice
    • General education — composition, social sciences, humanities
    • Technical electives — specialized courses that let students tailor the degree to a sub-specialty

    The College Admissions Process

    For most students, an engineering degree begins with applying to college. The college admissions process is the set of steps a student takes to apply for admission to a college or university, from researching programs to submitting applications to receiving and comparing offers.

    The major steps in a typical four-year university admissions process are:

    1. Research (Junior year and summer before senior year) — Identify 8–12 schools with ABET-accredited programs in your field, considering location, size, cost, and selectivity.
    2. Standardized tests — SAT or ACT scores; some engineering schools also consider SAT Subject Tests or AP exam scores favorably.
    3. Build your application — Transcripts, a personal statement or essays, a resume or activity list, and letters of recommendation from teachers or counselors who know your academic work.
    4. Apply (Fall of senior year) — Submit applications through the Common Application, Coalition Application, or individual school portals. Early Decision and Early Action deadlines are typically November 1–15; Regular Decision deadlines are usually January 1–February 1.
    5. Financial aid — Complete the FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) as soon as it opens each year (October 1). Many merit scholarships have separate deadlines.
    6. Decide (Spring of senior year) — Compare acceptance letters and financial aid packages; commit to one school by May 1 (National Decision Day).

    Engineering programs are often among the most competitive majors at a given school. Admissions committees look for strength in mathematics and science, evidence of problem-solving ability (coursework, competitions, projects), and genuine interest in the field.

    Professional Certifications

    A professional certification in engineering is a credential awarded by a professional organization that demonstrates a practitioner's competence in a specific area or technology — separate from a government license. Certifications are typically earned after passing an exam and sometimes after accumulating work experience.

    Unlike the PE license (which is government-issued and required for certain types of engineering practice), certifications are voluntary but valued by employers. Common examples include:

    • Project Management Professional (PMP) — awarded by PMI; relevant to engineers who manage projects
    • Certified Energy Manager (CEM) — for engineers working in energy efficiency
    • AWS Certified Solutions Architect — for computer and software engineers working with cloud infrastructure
    • LEED AP (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design Accredited Professional) — for civil and environmental engineers involved in sustainable building design

    Many professional societies also offer discipline-specific certifications for students and early-career engineers — a good way to demonstrate initiative before you have years of professional experience.

    Licensure Requirements

    Licensure is the legal authorization, issued by a state government, that permits an engineer to offer professional engineering services to the public. While most engineers who work for companies do not legally need a license, licensure is required whenever an engineer:

    • Signs and seals engineering drawings or reports that affect public safety
    • Works as an independent consultant or business owner
    • Works in civil, structural, geotechnical, or certain other public-works fields

    The licensure requirements for engineers in the United States follow a four-stage process overseen by the National Council of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying (NCEES) and administered by each state's engineering board.

    The four stages are:

    1. Education — Graduate from an ABET-accredited engineering program
    2. Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) Exam — Pass the computer-based exam offered by NCEES (this leads to the EIT credential)
    3. Progressive Experience — Accumulate at least four years of engineering experience under the supervision of a licensed PE
    4. Principles and Practice of Engineering (PE) Exam — Pass the discipline-specific PE exam

    The Engineer in Training (EIT) Exam

    The Engineer in Training (EIT) Exam — officially called the Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) Exam — is the first formal step on the licensure path. It is a computer-based, 5 hour and 20 minute exam administered by NCEES (National Council of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying) at approved testing centers.

    Most students take the FE Exam during their final year of college or shortly after graduation, while the foundational mathematics and science concepts are fresh. The exam is offered in several discipline-specific versions, including:

    • FE Chemical
    • FE Civil
    • FE Electrical and Computer
    • FE Environmental
    • FE Industrial and Systems
    • FE Mechanical
    • FE Other Disciplines (a general version)

    Passing the FE Exam earns the title Engineer in Training (EIT) or Engineer Intern (EI) depending on the state. The EIT designation is a formal acknowledgment that you have demonstrated competency in the scientific and mathematical foundations of engineering and are ready to begin gaining supervised professional experience.

    The Professional Engineer (PE) License

    The Professional Engineer (PE) License is the highest standard credential in the engineering profession. It is issued by state licensing boards after an engineer demonstrates education, experience, and competence through examination. Once licensed, a PE can stamp engineering documents, take legal responsibility for their designs, and offer engineering services independently.

    To receive a PE license, a candidate must have:

    1. A degree from an ABET-accredited program
    2. A passing FE Exam score (EIT status)
    3. Four years of progressive engineering experience under the direct supervision of a PE
    4. A passing score on the PE Exam in their discipline

    The PE Exam is an 8-hour exam (split into morning and afternoon sessions) administered by NCEES twice per year. It tests advanced, discipline-specific knowledge — significantly deeper than the FE Exam.

    Comparing Certifications and Licensure

    It is easy to confuse professional certifications with professional licensure. Both are credentials that signal competence, but they serve different functions and carry different legal weight. The following comparison clarifies the distinction.

    Feature Professional Certification PE License
    Issuing authority Professional association or vendor State government board
    Legal requirement Voluntary Required for certain public-safety work
    Exam Association-specific NCEES FE Exam → PE Exam
    Work experience required Varies (often 2–5 years) 4 years under PE supervision
    Renewal Annual or biennial, with continuing education Biennial, with PDH hours
    Portability National or international State-by-state (reciprocity agreements exist)

    Many engineers hold both certifications and a PE license — the certifications signal expertise in a specialty area, while the PE license signals professional accountability and legal authority.

    Chapter Summary

    This chapter introduced the landscape of engineering specializations and the pathways that lead from high school into professional engineering practice. Here is what you should be able to recall and explain after working through this material:

    • Engineering has dozens of specializations; your field of interest is where your skills, curiosity, and the world's needs intersect.
    • Career research requires multiple source types — government data, professional societies, real job postings, and conversations with practitioners.
    • Postsecondary options range from community college transfer tracks to four-year programs to graduate school, each with different timelines and costs.
    • ABET accreditation is the quality standard for engineering degree programs — check for it before committing to a school.
    • The licensure path follows a fixed sequence: ABET degree → FE Exam (EIT) → four years of supervised experience → PE Exam → PE License.
    • Professional certifications are voluntary, specialty-area credentials that complement the PE license but do not replace it.

    Your Engineering Field Exploration Lab will ask you to apply all of these concepts to one specific engineering field. Come to that assignment with a field already in mind — the self-assessment explorer above is a good starting point if you are undecided.
