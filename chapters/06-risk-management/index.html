    Chapter 6: Risk Management

    Summary

    Before committing to a design direction, engineers must evaluate whether their solution is safe and identify what could go wrong. This chapter teaches students to apply safety standards to their designs, systematically identify risks, analyze their likelihood and impact, and develop strategies to reduce or eliminate them — a critical step before the project proposal is written.

    Introduction

    Every engineering design carries some degree of risk. A bridge might fail under an unexpected load. A medical device might malfunction at a critical moment. A software system might leak private data. Engineers do not eliminate risk entirely — that is often impossible — but they are professionally and ethically obligated to identify risks, understand them, and reduce them to an acceptable level before any design is built or deployed.

    Risk management is not a bureaucratic formality. It is one of the most direct expressions of the engineering code of ethics you studied in Chapter 5: placing the safety, health, and welfare of the public above all other considerations. This chapter gives you the tools to do that work systematically for your own practicum project.

    Safety Standards

    A safety standard is a documented set of requirements, guidelines, or specifications that defines acceptable performance for an engineering design in a given context. Safety standards are developed by government agencies, professional engineering organizations, and industry bodies — not individual engineers — precisely because safety decisions should not depend on one person's judgment alone.

    Before you can evaluate your design against a standard, you need to know which standards apply to your project. The governing standard depends on the domain your project touches:

    Domain Example Standards Body Example Standard
    Electrical systems UL (Underwriters Laboratories), IEEE UL 62368-1 (audio/video equipment)
    Structural / civil ASCE (American Society of Civil Engineers) ASCE 7 (minimum design loads)
    Mechanical / pressure ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers) ASME B31.3 (process piping)
    Consumer products CPSC (Consumer Product Safety Commission) 16 CFR Part 1500 (hazardous substances)
    Environmental EPA National Ambient Air Quality Standards
    Medical devices FDA, ISO ISO 13485 (quality management for medical devices)

    For high school practicum projects, your design will rarely need to comply with a formal regulatory standard — but understanding which standard would apply in a real deployment helps you think like a professional engineer.

    Design Safety Evaluation

    Design safety evaluation is the process of systematically comparing a proposed engineering design against relevant safety standards and criteria to determine whether the design is acceptable before resources are committed to building it. Catching a safety problem at the design stage costs far less — in time, money, and potentially human harm — than discovering it after construction or deployment.

    The core technique is a structured checklist review. For each component or function of your design, you ask:

    • Does this component meet the applicable safety standard?
    • What happens if this component fails? Does failure cascade to other parts?
    • Are there guards, interlocks, or redundancies that prevent or contain failure?
    • Are the materials, tolerances, and energy levels within accepted safe ranges?

    Two of the most widely used formal safety evaluation methods in professional engineering are FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis) and FTA (Fault Tree Analysis). FMEA works bottom-up: you list every component, identify every way it could fail, and trace the effects of that failure through the system. FTA works top-down: you start with a catastrophic outcome you want to prevent and work backward to find combinations of events that could cause it. Both methods are more rigorous than a simple checklist, and both appear in engineering practice in regulated industries like aerospace, automotive, and medical devices.

    For your practicum project, a structured design safety checklist — rather than a full FMEA — is the appropriate tool. Your checklist should address materials safety, energy hazards, user interaction points, failure modes for critical components, and environmental impact.

    Risk Identification

    Before you can manage a risk, you must find it. Risk identification is the process of systematically discovering, naming, and documenting every potential event or condition that could negatively affect your project's outcome, timeline, budget, or safety. The goal is not to predict the future with certainty but to ensure that no significant threat goes unexamined.

    Effective risk identification draws on several techniques used together:

    • Brainstorming — Free, structured group discussion focused on "what could go wrong?" with no idea dismissed too early.
    • Assumption analysis — Reviewing every assumption your project plan makes (e.g., "the materials will arrive on time," "the motor will operate at the rated voltage") and asking what happens if that assumption is wrong.
    • Checklist review — Using a pre-built category list to prompt thinking in areas that brainstorming might miss.
    • Expert interviews — Asking someone with relevant experience what problems they have seen in similar projects.

    For a practicum project, four broad risk categories cover most threats you will encounter:

    Risk Category Examples
    Technical risks Design flaw discovered late, material behaves unexpectedly, software bug
    Schedule risks Supplier delay, school closure, unexpected complexity
    Resource risks Budget overrun, tool unavailable, needed expertise missing
    Safety risks Electrical hazard, sharp edge, chemical exposure, structural failure

    Every identified risk should be recorded in a risk register — a simple table that names the risk, describes its potential cause, and tracks its status throughout the project. The risk register becomes the working document you update as the project progresses.

    Risk Analysis

    Finding a risk is not enough — you also need to understand how serious it is. Risk analysis is the process of evaluating each identified risk on two dimensions: likelihood (how probable is it that this risk will occur?) and impact (how severe would the consequences be if it did occur?). Together, these two dimensions produce a risk score that tells you where to focus your attention.

    Both likelihood and impact are rated on a consistent scale. A common scale used in engineering practice assigns values from 1 to 5:

    Rating Likelihood Impact
    1 Rare — unlikely under normal conditions Negligible — minor inconvenience, easily corrected
    2 Unlikely — possible but not expected Minor — small delay or cost increase
    3 Possible — may occur in some circumstances Moderate — noticeable effect on schedule, budget, or quality
    4 Likely — expected to occur at some point Significant — major rework required or safety concern
    5 Almost certain — will probably occur Catastrophic — project failure or serious harm

    The risk score is the product of Likelihood × Impact. A risk rated Likelihood 4, Impact 3 has a score of 12, which is higher priority than a risk rated Likelihood 2, Impact 5 (score of 10) — even though the second risk would be more severe if it occurred.

    Risk Mitigation Strategies

    Knowing that a risk is high-priority tells you it needs attention — but not what to do about it. Risk mitigation strategies are the specific actions a project team takes to reduce the likelihood or impact of an identified risk, or to prepare for a fast recovery if the risk does occur.

    There are four fundamental strategies, and choosing the right one depends on the risk's nature, its score, and the resources available:

    • Avoid — Change the project plan to eliminate the risk entirely. Example: if using a high-voltage component creates a critical electrical hazard, redesign to use low-voltage alternatives.
    • Reduce — Take action to lower the likelihood or impact. Example: order materials two weeks early to reduce the impact of a supplier delay.
    • Transfer — Shift the consequence to another party. Example: purchase materials insurance, or use a vendor who guarantees replacement of defective parts.
    • Accept — Acknowledge the risk and decide to proceed without additional action, typically for low-scoring risks. Acceptance should always be a conscious, documented choice — not just an oversight.

    Most practicum projects will rely primarily on avoid and reduce strategies, because transfer options require contracts and financial resources that students typically do not control.

    For every high-priority risk (orange or red on your matrix), your risk management plan should document:

    1. The risk name and description
    2. Its likelihood and impact ratings
    3. The chosen mitigation strategy (avoid / reduce / transfer / accept)
    4. Specific action steps and who is responsible for each
    5. A contingency plan — what you will do if the risk occurs despite mitigation

    Putting It Together: Your Risk Management Plan

    Your Risk Management assignment asks you to apply these concepts directly to your practicum project. The deliverable is a completed risk register that covers at least six identified risks, with full analysis and mitigation strategies for any risk scoring 8 or higher.

    Use this sequence:

    1. Identify at least ten candidate risks across all four categories (technical, schedule, resource, safety).
    2. Rate each risk on the 1–5 likelihood and impact scales.
    3. Calculate the risk score and place each risk on the matrix.
    4. Select a mitigation strategy for every risk scoring 8 or higher.
    5. Document specific action steps and a contingency plan for each high-priority risk.
    6. Review the safety-specific risks with your teacher before finalizing your design direction.

    A well-prepared risk management plan does more than satisfy the assignment — it makes your proposal stronger, because a peer reviewer or judge can see that you have thought critically about what could go wrong and have a credible plan to address it.

    Chapter Summary

    Risk management is the discipline of finding problems before they find you. Here is what you should be able to explain after completing this chapter:

    • Safety standards are external, authoritative documents that define acceptable performance levels; they are not optional suggestions.
    • Design safety evaluation means checking your design against those standards before committing to building it — FMEA and FTA are the professional methods; a structured checklist is the appropriate starting point for this course.
    • Risk identification draws on brainstorming, assumption analysis, checklists, and expert input to surface threats across four categories: technical, schedule, resource, and safety.
    • Risk analysis scores each risk on Likelihood × Impact and visualizes the results on a risk matrix to set response priorities.
    • Risk mitigation strategies — avoid, reduce, transfer, or accept — translate your analysis into specific, assigned action steps.

    Your Risk Management assignment puts all five concepts into practice for your specific project. Complete it before your project proposal is written — your proposal will be stronger for it.
