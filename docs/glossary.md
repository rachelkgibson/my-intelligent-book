# Glossary of Terms

#### Brainstorming Solutions

A structured ideation technique in which all possible solutions to an engineering problem are generated without immediate evaluation or criticism, encouraging creative and divergent thinking before narrowing options.

Effective brainstorming separates idea generation from idea evaluation, producing a wider solution space than jumping directly to the first workable idea.

**Example:** Before settling on a bridge design, a team spends ten minutes listing every structural form they can think of — trusses, arches, cable-stays, beams — before evaluating any of them.

**See also:** Engineering Challenge Definition, Constraints and Criteria

---

#### Budget Development

The process of creating a comprehensive financial plan for a project by estimating all anticipated costs, organizing them by category, and producing a total expenditure forecast.

**Example:** A student developing a solar-powered irrigation system lists material costs ($85 for tubing and sensors), tool rental ($20), and a 10% contingency buffer to arrive at a total project budget of $115.

**See also:** Cost Estimation, Budget Research Methods, Resource Allocation

---

#### Budget Research Methods

Techniques used to gather accurate cost data for project planning, including supplier quotes, online price comparisons, catalog research, and consultation with professionals who have purchased similar materials.

Thorough cost research prevents underestimating a budget, which is one of the most common causes of project failure or scope reduction.

**Example:** A student researches the cost of PVC pipe on three supplier websites and calls a local hardware store for a bulk discount quote before entering a number in the project budget.

**See also:** Cost Estimation, Budget Development

---

#### Code of Ethics

A formal written document adopted by a professional organization that articulates the values, duties, and behavioral standards expected of all members practicing in that field.

**Example:** The National Society of Professional Engineers (NSPE) Code of Ethics states that engineers must hold the safety, health, and welfare of the public paramount above all other obligations.

**See also:** Engineering Ethics, Professional Responsibility

---

#### College Admissions Process

The sequence of steps through which a student applies for acceptance to a college or university, including researching institutions, submitting applications, providing transcripts and test scores, and comparing acceptance offers.

**Example:** A student researching ABET-accredited mechanical engineering programs creates a spreadsheet comparing application deadlines, required GPA, SAT scores, and tuition costs across eight universities.

**See also:** Postsecondary Education Options, Engineering Programs, Degree Requirements

---

#### Conflict of Interest

A situation in which an engineer's personal financial, relational, or professional interests could inappropriately influence — or appear to influence — a decision made in their professional capacity.

Disclosing and managing conflicts of interest is a core obligation in professional engineering codes of ethics.

**Example:** An engineer who owns stock in a construction materials company and is also reviewing bids for a public bridge project has a conflict of interest that must be disclosed before proceeding.

**See also:** Engineering Ethics, Personal vs Professional Ethics

---

#### Constraints and Criteria

The boundaries and performance targets that define an acceptable engineering solution. Criteria specify what a solution must accomplish; constraints specify limits — such as cost, time, material, or physical boundaries — within which it must operate.

**Example:** For a water filtration project, the criteria might be "removes 99% of particulates" while the constraints are "costs under $50 to build" and "fits in a 12-inch diameter tube."

**See also:** Engineering Challenge Definition, Design Safety Evaluation

---

#### Constructive Feedback

Specific, evidence-based observations about another person's work that identify both strengths and areas for improvement in a way that is actionable and respectful.

Constructive feedback is more valuable than vague praise or criticism because it gives the recipient clear information they can act on.

**Example:** Instead of saying "your proposal was confusing," a peer reviewer writes "the problem statement on page 2 does not explain who is affected by the problem — adding that would strengthen the need."

**See also:** Peer Review Process, Proposal Review Criteria

---

#### Copyrights

A form of intellectual property protection that gives the creator of an original work — such as written text, software code, artwork, or music — the exclusive legal right to reproduce, distribute, and display that work.

Unlike patents, copyright protection arises automatically at the moment a work is created and does not require registration, though registration strengthens enforcement.

**Example:** A student who writes original code for a sensor monitoring system automatically holds copyright over that code; others cannot copy and publish it without permission.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, Patents, Trademarks, Trade Secrets

---

#### Cost Estimation

The process of forecasting the total financial expenditure required to complete a project, based on material quantities, labor, equipment, and other resource requirements.

**Example:** Using a bill of materials and current supplier prices, a student estimates that their prototype will cost $78 in raw materials plus $15 in tools, for a total estimated cost of $93.

**See also:** Budget Research Methods, Budget Development, Cost-Benefit Analysis

---

#### Cost-Benefit Analysis

A systematic comparison of the total expected costs of a project or decision against the total expected benefits, used to determine whether proceeding is justified.

**Example:** A student calculating whether to add Bluetooth connectivity to a project estimates $30 in added parts cost and 4 hours of added development time against the benefit of remote monitoring — and decides the benefit does not justify the cost for the scope of the practicum.

**See also:** Cost Estimation, Budget Development

---

#### Cover Letter Writing

The process of composing a one-page professional letter, submitted alongside a resume, that explains why an applicant is interested in a specific position and connects their experience to the employer's stated needs.

**Example:** A student applying for a summer engineering internship writes a cover letter that references the company's recent bridge rehabilitation project and explains how their CAD coursework directly applies to the role.

**See also:** Resume Writing, Job Application Process, Work Portfolio

---

#### Degree Requirements

The specific combination of courses, credit hours, grade thresholds, and other academic milestones that a student must complete to earn a particular academic credential from an educational institution.

**Example:** An ABET-accredited Bachelor of Science in Civil Engineering typically requires approximately 128 credit hours including calculus through differential equations, statics, fluid mechanics, and a senior capstone design course.

**See also:** Engineering Programs, Postsecondary Education Options

---

#### Design Optimization

The iterative process of modifying a design to improve its performance against one or more criteria — such as strength, efficiency, cost, or weight — while remaining within all constraints.

**Example:** After initial testing shows a support beam deflects too much under load, a student increases its cross-sectional area and repositions a support point, reducing deflection by 40% without exceeding the cost constraint.

**See also:** Testing and Iteration, Prototype Development, Constraints and Criteria

---

#### Design Safety Evaluation

The systematic review of an engineering design to identify features that could cause harm to users, bystanders, or the environment, measured against established safety standards.

**Example:** Before finalizing a student-built conveyor system, the team reviews each moving part for pinch points, checks that all electrical connections are insulated, and verifies the emergency stop is reachable from both ends of the device.

**See also:** Safety Standards, Risk Identification, Public Safety in Engineering

---

#### Documentation Standards

Agreed-upon rules and formats for recording engineering work — including notation, units, file naming, version control, and required content — that ensure records are consistent, complete, and usable by others.

**Example:** A team adopts the standard that every design file must include the author's name, a revision number, and a date in the title block before it can be shared with the project mentor.

**See also:** Technical Report Writing, Report Structure

---

#### Engineer in Training (EIT) Exam

A computer-based national examination administered by NCEES that tests a candidate's mastery of the fundamental mathematics and sciences underlying all engineering disciplines, typically taken near the end of an undergraduate engineering program.

Passing the EIT Exam is the first formal step on the path to obtaining a Professional Engineer license. Candidates who pass receive the EIT or Engineer Intern (EI) designation.

**Example:** A senior in a mechanical engineering program registers for the FE Mechanical exam in October, studying fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, and dynamics in the months prior to the test date.

**See also:** Professional Engineer (PE) License, Licensure Requirements

---

#### Engineering Career Research

The systematic process of gathering and evaluating information about what engineers in a specific field do, what qualifications they hold, how much they earn, and what the employment outlook for that field is.

**Example:** A student uses the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook to compare median salaries, required education levels, and ten-year job growth projections for civil and biomedical engineering.

**See also:** Engineering Specializations, Field of Interest Selection

---

#### Engineering Challenge Definition

A precise written statement that describes the problem an engineering solution must address, identifies who is affected, specifies the boundaries of the problem space, and distinguishes the problem from potential solutions.

Clearly defining the challenge before proposing solutions prevents teams from solving the wrong problem or building solutions in search of a need.

**Example:** Instead of "build a better water bottle," a well-formed engineering challenge definition reads: "Design a portable hydration device for trail runners that maintains liquid temperature within 5°F of initial temperature for four hours without increasing pack weight by more than 8 oz."

**See also:** Problem Identification, Needs Assessment, Constraints and Criteria

---

#### Engineering Design Process

A structured, iterative methodology used by engineers to move from a recognized need or problem through research, ideation, prototyping, testing, and refinement toward a solution that meets specified criteria and constraints.

Unlike the scientific method, the engineering design process is fundamentally solution-focused and accepts multiple valid outcomes rather than seeking a single correct answer.

**Example:** A student follows the engineering design process by defining a problem, researching existing solutions, brainstorming designs, building a prototype, testing it against success criteria, and improving the design based on test results.

**See also:** Scientific Method, Engineering vs Science, Prototype Development

---

#### Engineering Ethics

The application of ethical principles and professional standards to the practice of engineering, governing how engineers make decisions, treat clients, protect the public, and maintain honesty in their work.

**Example:** An engineer who discovers a structural flaw in a design during construction is ethically obligated to report it and halt work, even if doing so delays the project and increases costs for the client.

**See also:** Code of Ethics, Professional Responsibility, Public Safety in Engineering, Ethical Decision Making

---

#### Engineering Internships

Structured, typically paid work experiences in which engineering students work at a company or organization under the supervision of licensed engineers, applying classroom knowledge to real projects for a defined period.

**Example:** A student completes a ten-week summer internship at a water treatment facility, assisting engineers with sensor calibration and writing a summary report on a filtration system upgrade.

**See also:** Job Application Process, Professional Readiness

---

#### Engineering Programs

Formally structured curricula at colleges and universities that lead to a degree in a specific engineering discipline, typically accredited by ABET to ensure they meet national standards for content and rigor.

**Example:** Virginia Tech's Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering offers an ABET-accredited B.S. in Civil Engineering that includes required courses in structural analysis, transportation, and a senior design capstone.

**See also:** Degree Requirements, Postsecondary Education Options

---

#### Engineering Specializations

The distinct sub-disciplines within the engineering profession, each focused on a particular type of system, material, or problem domain — such as civil, mechanical, electrical, chemical, or biomedical engineering.

**Example:** A student interested in medical devices researches biomedical engineering as a specialization, learning that practitioners design prosthetics, imaging systems, and diagnostic equipment.

**See also:** Engineering Career Research, Field of Interest Selection

---

#### Engineering vs Science

The distinction between two related but fundamentally different disciplines: science seeks to explain natural phenomena through observation and experimentation, while engineering applies scientific knowledge to design and build solutions to human problems.

**Example:** A scientist studies why certain materials conduct heat more efficiently; an engineer uses that knowledge to design a more effective heat sink for a computer processor.

**See also:** Scientific Method, Engineering Design Process

---

#### Ethical Decision Making

The process of identifying when a situation raises ethical concerns, evaluating available courses of action against moral principles and professional standards, and choosing the option that best upholds those principles.

**Example:** An engineer asked to certify a design she has not personally reviewed must decide whether to refuse the request, request more time to review, or — unethically — sign without reviewing; applying the NSPE code, she requests time to review before certifying.

**See also:** Engineering Ethics, Code of Ethics, Conflict of Interest

---

#### Feedback Loops

Mechanisms within a system in which a portion of the system's output is routed back as input, influencing the system's future behavior. Negative feedback loops stabilize a system; positive feedback loops amplify change.

**Example:** A thermostat is a negative feedback loop: when room temperature rises above the set point, the system turns on cooling, which lowers the temperature back toward the target.

**See also:** Systems Thinking, System Inputs and Outputs, Subsystem Interactions

---

#### Field of Interest Selection

The process by which a student or early-career professional identifies the engineering specialization that best aligns with their personal strengths, academic interests, and long-term career goals.

**Example:** After completing a self-assessment comparing her enjoyment of biology versus physics coursework, a student selects biomedical engineering as her primary field of interest for further research.

**See also:** Engineering Specializations, Engineering Career Research

---

#### Final Project Presentation

A formal oral and visual presentation in which a student communicates the problem addressed, the design decisions made, the results of testing, and the outcomes of their completed engineering project to an audience of evaluators and stakeholders.

**Example:** At the end of the practicum, a student presents a ten-minute slide deck to a panel of engineers and community members, demonstrating how her water quality monitoring device was designed, built, tested, and improved.

**See also:** Presentation Skills, Visual Communication, Stakeholder Communication

---

#### Gantt Chart

A horizontal bar chart used in project management to display a project schedule, showing each task as a bar whose length represents its duration and whose position on a timeline indicates its start and end dates.

**Example:** A student creates a Gantt chart showing that "Materials Procurement" runs from weeks 1–2, "Prototype Build" from weeks 3–5, "Testing" in weeks 6–7, and "Final Report" in weeks 8–9.

**See also:** Schedule Development, Milestone Planning, Work Schedule Creation, Scheduling Tools

---

#### Hypothesis Testing

The scientific process of formulating a specific, falsifiable prediction about the outcome of an experiment, then designing and conducting that experiment to determine whether the data support or contradict the prediction.

**Example:** A student hypothesizes that increasing the fin angle on a water rocket from 15° to 30° will increase altitude. She launches three rockets at each angle and records the results to test the hypothesis.

**See also:** Scientific Method, Engineering vs Science

---

#### Intellectual Property

Creations of the mind — such as inventions, artistic works, brand identifiers, and confidential business information — that are protected by law, giving creators exclusive rights to use and benefit from their creations for a defined period.

**Example:** The unique algorithm a student develops to process sensor data in her practicum project may qualify as intellectual property that she could protect through copyright (for the code) or a patent (for the underlying method).

**See also:** Patents, Copyrights, Trademarks, Trade Secrets, IP Protection and Enforcement

---

#### Interview Skills

The set of communication, preparation, and professional behavior competencies that enable a job candidate to effectively convey their qualifications, experience, and character during a formal employment conversation.

**Example:** A student practices the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to answer behavioral interview questions, structuring each answer around a specific past experience with a measurable outcome.

**See also:** Mock Interview Practice, Job Application Process

---

#### IP Infringement

The unauthorized use, reproduction, distribution, or modification of intellectual property that is protected by patent, copyright, trademark, or trade secret law, without the rights holder's permission.

**Example:** A student who copies a proprietary software library into her project without purchasing a license or verifying that it carries an open-source license is committing copyright infringement.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, IP Protection and Enforcement

---

#### IP Protection and Enforcement

The legal mechanisms and procedures used to secure intellectual property rights — such as filing for a patent or registering a trademark — and to prevent or remedy unauthorized use of those rights through legal action.

**Example:** A startup that invented a new type of water filtration membrane files a utility patent application with the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office to prevent competitors from manufacturing the same design.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, IP Infringement, Patents

---

#### Job Application Process

The structured sequence of actions a candidate takes to pursue employment, from identifying an opening and submitting application materials through interviews and a final offer, including navigation of employer screening systems.

**Example:** A student identifies an internship on LinkedIn, tailors her resume to match keywords in the job posting, submits her application through the company's online portal, and prepares for a phone screen the following week.

**See also:** Resume Writing, Cover Letter Writing, Interview Skills, Engineering Internships

---

#### Licensure Requirements

The conditions an engineer must satisfy — typically including an accredited degree, a passing score on the Fundamentals of Engineering exam, a defined period of supervised work experience, and a passing score on the Principles and Practice exam — before a state board will issue a Professional Engineer license.

**See also:** Professional Engineer (PE) License, Engineer in Training (EIT) Exam

---

#### Milestone Planning

The process of identifying and scheduling specific, measurable checkpoints within a project timeline that mark the completion of a significant phase or deliverable, enabling progress tracking and early detection of schedule risks.

**Example:** A student sets three milestones for her practicum project: "Prototype assembled" by week 4, "Testing complete" by week 7, and "Final report submitted" by week 10, each with a specific deliverable that marks completion.

**See also:** Schedule Development, Gantt Chart, Work Schedule Creation

---

#### Mock Interview Practice

A structured rehearsal exercise in which a student simulates a formal employment interview with a teacher, peer, or professional volunteer serving as the interviewer, followed by specific written or verbal feedback.

Research consistently shows that candidates who practice answering questions aloud perform significantly better in real interviews than those who only prepare mentally.

**Example:** Before the Professional Mock Interview Event, a student rehearses with a classmate using a list of ten common engineering interview questions, recording the session to review her pacing and eye contact.

**See also:** Interview Skills, Job Application Process

---

#### Modeling Techniques

Methods used to create physical, mathematical, or computational representations of a system or design, allowing engineers to study, analyze, and communicate how the system behaves without building the full-scale version.

**Example:** Before building a full roof structure, a student creates a scaled physical model using balsa wood and tests it under a simulated wind load to predict how the actual structure will perform.

**See also:** Simulation Software, Prototype Development

---

#### Needs Assessment

A systematic process of gathering and analyzing information about the gap between a current condition and a desired condition, used to confirm that a problem is real, significant, and worth addressing before a design solution is pursued.

**Example:** Before designing a school garden irrigation system, a student surveys student gardeners to document how frequently plants die from under-watering and estimates how much time is currently spent on manual watering.

**See also:** Problem Identification, Opportunity Recognition, Engineering Challenge Definition

---

#### Opportunity Recognition

The ability to identify situations where an unmet need, an inefficiency, or an emerging capability could be addressed through an engineering solution, even when no one has explicitly stated a problem.

**Example:** After noticing that wheelchair users at school cannot independently open the heavy library doors, a student recognizes an opportunity to design a low-cost automatic door assist that does not require electrical renovation.

**See also:** Problem Identification, Needs Assessment

---

#### Patents

Exclusive rights granted by a government to an inventor for a limited period — typically 20 years for utility patents — that prevent others from making, using, or selling the patented invention without the inventor's permission.

**Example:** If a student invents a novel method for removing microplastics from drinking water, she could file a utility patent application to protect that invention and prevent competitors from commercializing the same method.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, Copyrights, Trademarks, Trade Secrets

---

#### Peer Review Process

A formal evaluation procedure in which qualified reviewers — peers with relevant knowledge — assess a work product against defined criteria and provide structured, documented feedback to improve its quality.

In engineering, peer review is used at multiple project stages: reviewing designs before fabrication, proposals before submission, and reports before publication.

**Example:** Each student in the class reads and scores two classmates' project proposals using the same rubric the teacher will use, then submits written comments to the proposal authors.

**See also:** Proposal Review Criteria, Constructive Feedback, Proposal Defense

---

#### Personal vs Professional Ethics

The distinction between the moral values and principles that guide an individual's private decisions (personal ethics) and the formal standards of conduct that govern behavior in a professional role (professional ethics), which may sometimes conflict.

**Example:** An engineer's personal belief is that a client's project is wasteful, but his professional ethics require him to provide honest, competent service regardless of personal opinion — so he completes the project while ensuring it meets all safety standards.

**See also:** Engineering Ethics, Conflict of Interest, Code of Ethics

---

#### Portfolio Components

The specific categories of materials included in a work portfolio, such as a personal statement, resume, project descriptions, work samples, certifications, and reflective writing, collectively chosen to demonstrate the breadth and depth of a candidate's skills.

**Example:** A student assembles her portfolio with a personal statement, a one-page resume, three project write-ups with photographs, her OSHA 10 certificate, and a one-page reflection on her most challenging design experience.

**See also:** Work Portfolio

---

#### Postsecondary Education Options

The range of formal educational pathways available after high school graduation, including two-year community colleges, four-year universities, technical institutes, and graduate programs, each offering different credentials and timelines relevant to engineering careers.

**Example:** A student who is unsure about committing to a four-year engineering program researches the Virginia Community College System's guaranteed transfer agreements that allow students to begin at a community college and transfer to an engineering school after two years.

**See also:** Engineering Programs, Degree Requirements, College Admissions Process

---

#### Presentation Skills

The abilities required to effectively communicate information to an audience through spoken language, body language, pacing, and engagement, in a way that is clear, credible, and appropriately adapted to the context and audience.

**Example:** A student practices projecting her voice, making eye contact with multiple sections of the audience, and pausing after key points to allow the audience to absorb information before moving to the next slide.

**See also:** Visual Communication, Final Project Presentation, Stakeholder Communication

---

#### Problem Identification

The process of recognizing and articulating a specific, observable need, deficiency, or failure in a current system or situation that an engineering intervention could address.

**Example:** After observing that students frequently trip on a cracked section of the school's outdoor walkway, a student identifies the problem as "a deteriorated pedestrian surface that creates a fall hazard."

**See also:** Needs Assessment, Opportunity Recognition, Engineering Challenge Definition

---

#### Professional Certifications

Credentials awarded by professional associations or credentialing bodies that formally recognize a practitioner's expertise in a specific technical area or skill, typically earned by passing an exam and sometimes requiring documented work experience.

**Example:** A civil engineer specializing in sustainable construction earns the LEED AP credential by passing an exam administered by the U.S. Green Building Council, signaling expertise in green building design and certification.

**See also:** Licensure Requirements, Professional Engineer (PE) License

---

#### Professional Engineer (PE) License

A government-issued credential that authorizes an engineer to offer professional engineering services to the public, sign and seal engineering documents, and take legal responsibility for engineering work, earned by meeting education, experience, and examination requirements.

**Example:** A structural engineer who has passed both the FE and PE exams and worked four years under a licensed PE can stamp the drawings for a bridge renovation project, certifying that the design meets safety standards.

**See also:** Engineer in Training (EIT) Exam, Licensure Requirements

---

#### Professional Readiness

The degree to which an individual has developed the technical skills, professional behaviors, communication abilities, and ethical judgment needed to function effectively in a workplace engineering role.

**Example:** At the end of the practicum course, a student demonstrates professional readiness by submitting a technical report on time, presenting confidently to a stakeholder panel, and incorporating peer feedback without being asked.

**See also:** Interview Skills, Work Portfolio, Final Project Presentation

---

#### Professional Responsibility

The obligation of a licensed or practicing engineer to act competently, honestly, and in the public interest, including the duty to acknowledge the limits of one's expertise and to report unsafe conditions.

**Example:** An engineer who realizes partway through a project that it requires geotechnical expertise she does not have has a professional responsibility to bring in a qualified specialist rather than proceed beyond her competency.

**See also:** Code of Ethics, Engineering Ethics, Public Safety in Engineering

---

#### Project Management

The application of processes, methods, and tools to plan, organize, monitor, and control the work of a team or individual to achieve a defined goal within agreed-upon time, cost, and quality constraints.

**Example:** A student uses project management practices to define her practicum's deliverables, assign herself weekly tasks, track material spending against her budget, and document risks before they become problems.

**See also:** Work Breakdown Structure, Gantt Chart, Milestone Planning, Schedule Development

---

#### Project Management Software

Digital tools designed to help individuals or teams plan tasks, assign responsibilities, track progress, manage budgets, and visualize project timelines, often replacing or supplementing manual scheduling methods.

**Example:** A student uses Microsoft Project to create a Gantt chart for her practicum, linking dependent tasks so that a delay in prototype fabrication automatically shifts the testing phase on the timeline.

**See also:** Gantt Chart, Work Schedule Creation, Project Management

---

#### Project Proposal Structure

The standard organizational format for a written engineering project proposal, typically including an executive summary, problem statement, background research, proposed solution, methodology, schedule, budget, and evaluation criteria.

**Example:** A student follows the required proposal structure by opening with a one-paragraph summary, then devoting separate sections to the need she identified, her proposed water filtration approach, a step-by-step design methodology, a ten-week timeline, and a $120 budget.

**See also:** Proposal Writing, Proposal Review Criteria

---

#### Proposal Defense

The formal presentation and question-and-answer session in which an engineer or student presents a project proposal to an evaluating audience, responds to questions and challenges, and demonstrates sufficient preparation and knowledge to justify proceeding.

**Example:** A student presents her five-minute proposal pitch and then answers five minutes of questions from a panel including her teacher and two engineers, responding to a challenge about her cost estimate with documented supplier quotes.

**See also:** Proposal Writing, Peer Review Process, Presentation Skills

---

#### Proposal Review Criteria

The specific, measurable standards against which a project proposal is evaluated during peer or instructor review, typically addressing completeness, feasibility, clarity of problem statement, soundness of methodology, and realism of schedule and budget.

**Example:** The review rubric for the practicum proposal awards up to 20 points each for problem statement clarity, solution feasibility, budget accuracy, schedule realism, and quality of written communication.

**See also:** Peer Review Process, Constructive Feedback, Project Proposal Structure

---

#### Proposal Writing

The process of composing a formal written document that describes a proposed engineering project, making the case for its value and feasibility by clearly articulating the problem, the proposed solution, the methodology, the timeline, and the anticipated outcomes.

**Example:** A student writes a five-page proposal explaining why a rain-collection system is needed at her school's garden, how she plans to build it, what materials she needs, and how she will evaluate whether it meets its performance targets.

**See also:** Project Proposal Structure, Proposal Defense

---

#### Prototype Development

The process of building a physical, digital, or scaled model of a proposed engineering solution in order to test its functionality, identify design flaws, and gather data for improvement before committing to a final design.

**Example:** A student builds a working prototype of her automated plant-watering system using low-cost components, discovering during construction that the pump produces too much pressure and selecting a lower-flow model for the next iteration.

**See also:** Testing and Iteration, Design Optimization, Engineering Design Process

---

#### Public Safety in Engineering

The principle that engineers bear a primary obligation to protect the physical safety, health, and welfare of the public in all design and professional decisions, superseding client or employer preferences when a genuine conflict arises.

**Example:** An engineer who discovers that a client wants to omit a code-required fire suppression system to reduce costs must refuse and explain that public safety requirements are not negotiable.

**See also:** Engineering Ethics, Professional Responsibility, Safety Standards

---

#### Report Structure

The conventional organizational framework for a technical engineering report, including sections such as title page, abstract, introduction, background, methodology, results, discussion, conclusions, and references.

**Example:** A student formats her final project report with a one-paragraph abstract summarizing the key finding, followed by separate sections for her problem statement, design process, test results, and lessons learned.

**See also:** Technical Report Writing, Documentation Standards

---

#### Resource Allocation

The process of identifying, assigning, and scheduling the people, materials, equipment, time, and money needed for each task in a project, ensuring that resources are available when needed without overcommitting or leaving gaps.

**Example:** A student allocates two hours per week for design work, identifies that she needs a specific drill bit that is only available in the school lab on Thursdays, and schedules her fabrication tasks accordingly.

**See also:** Project Management, Budget Development, Work Breakdown Structure

---

#### Resume Writing

The process of composing a concise, one-page professional document that summarizes an applicant's education, technical skills, work experience, and relevant achievements using action verbs and quantifiable results, tailored for a specific audience.

**Example:** A student replaces the resume bullet "helped with robot project" with "designed and fabricated the chassis frame for a 12 lb competitive robot that won first place in the regional obstacle course event."

**See also:** Cover Letter Writing, Work Portfolio, Job Application Process

---

#### Risk Analysis

The process of evaluating identified risks by estimating the probability that each risk event will occur and the severity of its impact if it does, typically represented using a likelihood-by-impact matrix.

**Example:** A student plots each of her project's risks on a 5×5 risk matrix, concluding that "motor failure during final presentation" is high-impact but low-probability and "running over budget" is medium-impact and high-probability.

**See also:** Risk Identification, Risk Mitigation Strategies

---

#### Risk Identification

The systematic process of recognizing and documenting potential events or conditions that could negatively affect a project's schedule, cost, quality, or safety before they occur.

**Example:** During a project planning session, a student lists eight potential risks including "supplier discontinues needed component," "prototype takes longer to build than estimated," and "test environment is unavailable on scheduled date."

**See also:** Risk Analysis, Risk Mitigation Strategies, Design Safety Evaluation

---

#### Risk Mitigation Strategies

Planned actions taken to reduce the probability that a risk event will occur, lessen the impact if it does occur, or transfer the risk to another party, developed in response to the findings of a risk analysis.

**Example:** To mitigate the risk of a key component being unavailable, a student orders materials three weeks early and identifies an alternative supplier as a backup.

**See also:** Risk Identification, Risk Analysis

---

#### Safety Standards

Formally established technical requirements, specifications, or codes — developed by regulatory agencies, government bodies, or professional standards organizations — that define minimum acceptable levels of safety for engineering designs, materials, and processes.

**Example:** A student designing an electrical component for her project references the National Electrical Code (NEC) to ensure all wiring is rated for the correct voltage and uses approved connectors.

**See also:** Design Safety Evaluation, Public Safety in Engineering

---

#### Schedule Development

The process of defining the sequence of project tasks, estimating the duration of each task, assigning resources, and producing a formal project timeline that shows when each activity will start and finish.

**Example:** A student sequences her project tasks by determining which activities depend on prior ones — she cannot begin testing until the prototype is built — and then assigns calendar dates to each task to produce a complete schedule.

**See also:** Gantt Chart, Milestone Planning, Work Schedule Creation, Scheduling Tools

---

#### Scheduling Tools

Software applications, templates, or systems used to create, display, and update project schedules, including Gantt chart software, calendar applications, and project management platforms.

**Example:** A student uses Google Sheets to build a simple Gantt chart for her ten-week project, color-coding phases and updating task completion percentages each Monday.

**See also:** Gantt Chart, Project Management Software, Schedule Development

---

#### Scientific Method

A systematic approach to investigating natural phenomena that involves observation, formulation of a testable hypothesis, controlled experimentation, data collection, analysis, and conclusion to produce reproducible, verifiable knowledge.

**Example:** A student uses the scientific method to determine which bridge adhesive is strongest by testing five samples of each adhesive type under identical conditions and recording the load at failure for each.

**See also:** Hypothesis Testing, Engineering vs Science, Engineering Design Process

---

#### Simulation Software

Computer programs that model the behavior of a physical system under defined conditions, allowing engineers to predict how a design will perform without building a physical prototype.

**Example:** A student uses free finite element analysis software to simulate how a beam in her design will deflect under a 50-pound load, identifying a stress concentration that she then addresses by adding a fillet radius.

**See also:** Modeling Techniques, Prototype Development

---

#### Stakeholder Communication

The process of sharing relevant project information with individuals or groups who have an interest in or are affected by the project, in a format and at a frequency appropriate to their role and level of technical knowledge.

**Example:** A student sends a brief weekly email to her project mentor summarizing what she completed, what obstacles she encountered, and what she plans to do the following week.

**See also:** Stakeholder Identification, Presentation Skills, Final Project Presentation

---

#### Stakeholder Identification

The process of recognizing and documenting all individuals, groups, or organizations that have a stake in the outcome of a project — including those who will use the solution, those who fund it, those affected by it, and those with authority over it.

**Example:** For a school garden irrigation project, a student identifies stakeholders including the science teacher who oversees the garden, students who maintain it, the principal who must approve construction, and the facilities department responsible for water access.

**See also:** Stakeholder Communication

---

#### Subsystem Interactions

The relationships and exchanges between the individual functional components of a larger system, where the behavior or output of one subsystem affects the inputs or performance of one or more other subsystems.

**Example:** In an automated greenhouse, the climate control subsystem (heating and cooling) and the moisture monitoring subsystem interact: when the heater runs, it lowers relative humidity, which triggers the irrigation subsystem to water more frequently.

**See also:** Systems Thinking, Feedback Loops, System Inputs and Outputs

---

#### System Inputs and Outputs

The resources, information, energy, or materials that enter a system (inputs) and the products, services, waste, or information that leave a system as a result of its processes (outputs).

**Example:** For a solar-powered water pump system: inputs include sunlight, water from a source, and electrical energy from the panel; outputs include pumped water delivered to a tank and heat dissipated from the motor.

**See also:** Systems Thinking, Feedback Loops, Subsystem Interactions

---

#### Systems Thinking

An analytical framework that emphasizes understanding a situation by examining the relationships, interactions, and feedback mechanisms among the components of a whole system, rather than analyzing individual parts in isolation.

Systems thinking is especially valuable in engineering because most real-world problems involve complex interdependencies where changing one variable produces unexpected effects elsewhere.

**Example:** A student applying systems thinking to a school energy reduction project examines not just lighting fixtures, but how lighting levels affect HVAC loads, student performance, and maintenance schedules — recognizing that changing one element shifts the others.

**See also:** System Inputs and Outputs, Feedback Loops, Subsystem Interactions

---

#### Technical Report Writing

The process of composing a formal document that communicates the methods, findings, and conclusions of an engineering project or investigation to a professional audience, using precise language, standard structure, and appropriate visual aids.

**Example:** A student writes a ten-page final project report including an abstract, a problem statement, a description of her design process, photographs and data from testing, a discussion of results, and a conclusion recommending design improvements.

**See also:** Report Structure, Documentation Standards

---

#### Testing and Iteration

The cyclical process of evaluating a prototype against defined performance criteria, identifying where and why it falls short, modifying the design, and retesting — repeated until the design meets all criteria or a decision is made to proceed with the best available version.

**Example:** A student's first prototype of a water filtration system removes 70% of particulates but the target is 95%; she adjusts the filter media and pore size, retests, and achieves 93% — then makes one more adjustment to reach 96%.

**See also:** Prototype Development, Design Optimization

---

#### Trade Secrets

Confidential business information — such as formulas, processes, designs, or customer lists — that gives a company a competitive advantage and is protected by keeping it secret rather than by registration with a government agency.

**Example:** The specific blend of materials in a manufacturer's proprietary composite building panel is a trade secret protected by requiring employees and partners to sign non-disclosure agreements rather than by filing a patent.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, Patents

---

#### Trademarks

Words, symbols, logos, slogans, or other identifiers that distinguish the goods or services of one organization from those of others, protected by law to prevent consumer confusion about the source of a product.

**Example:** The distinctive blue and gold color scheme and wordmark of a university's engineering program is a registered trademark; a student cannot sell merchandise using those marks without a licensing agreement.

**See also:** Intellectual Property, Copyrights

---

#### Visual Communication

The use of images, diagrams, charts, graphs, infographics, and other non-textual elements to convey information, relationships, or data in a way that is more immediately understandable or persuasive than text alone.

**Example:** Instead of describing her sensor readings in paragraphs, a student creates a line graph overlaying temperature and humidity data over a 48-hour period, making the correlation between the two variables immediately visible.

**See also:** Presentation Skills, Final Project Presentation

---

#### Work Breakdown Structure

A hierarchical decomposition of a project into progressively smaller, manageable components — from the overall project goal down through major phases, deliverables, and individual work packages — used to ensure all project work is identified and assigned.

**Example:** A student's WBS breaks her water purification project into three major phases (Design, Build, Test), each further divided into specific tasks such as "Select filter media," "Fabricate housing," and "Run particle count test."

**See also:** Project Management, Schedule Development, Resource Allocation

---

#### Work Portfolio

A curated collection of an individual's best work samples, credentials, and professional documents assembled to demonstrate their skills, growth, and achievements to a prospective employer, educational institution, or other evaluator.

**Example:** A student assembles a digital portfolio on Google Sites containing her resume, design drawings from three class projects, a photo documentation of her practicum prototype, and a written reflection on her engineering growth over four years.

**See also:** Portfolio Components, Resume Writing

---

#### Work Schedule Creation

The process of translating a project's task list and timeline into a structured, actionable calendar that specifies what work will be done on which days, by whom, and in what sequence.

**Example:** A student creates a week-by-week work schedule that assigns specific tasks to each of her ten project weeks, ensuring that materials are ordered before fabrication begins and testing is completed before the report is due.

**See also:** Gantt Chart, Schedule Development, Milestone Planning
