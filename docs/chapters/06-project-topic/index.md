## Introduction

You are not writing a full problem statement yet — that comes in Assignment 7. Right now, you are generating ideas broadly and choosing the one direction you want to commit to. The goal is to come into group formation with a clear, well-thought-out project topic that you can advocate for and build on.

Think back to Assignment 1. You researched an engineering field and the kinds of problems engineers in that field work on. This assignment is your chance to connect that research to something real and personal — a problem you have seen, experienced, or care about.

## What Makes a Good Practicum Project Topic?

Not every idea makes a good practicum project. Before you start generating ideas, understand the criteria a good topic must meet.

A strong practicum project topic is:

| Criteria | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Feasible** | Can be meaningfully designed and prototyped within one semester with the resources available at school |
| **Engineering-driven** | Requires you to design, build, or program something — not just research or report |
| **Problem-focused** | Addresses a real, observable problem affecting real people |
| **Interesting to you** | You will spend months on this — pick something you actually want to work on |
| **Appropriately scoped** | Not so big it's impossible, not so small there's nothing to design |

!!! warning "Watch Out for These Common Pitfalls"
    - **Too broad:** "I want to help the environment" is not a project — it's a value. "I want to design an automated composting monitor for the school garden" is a project.
    - **Too small:** "I want to make a better pencil holder" may not have enough engineering complexity for a semester-long project.
    - **Already solved:** If a $20 product already does exactly what you're describing, consider a harder version of the problem, or a version that serves a different population.

## Brainstorming Project Ideas

Good ideas come from paying attention. Engineers notice problems that other people walk past. Use the following three lenses to generate your initial list of ideas.

### Lens 1: Problems You Have Experienced

Think about your daily life, school, home, or community. What is frustrating, slow, inefficient, unsafe, or inaccessible?

Ask yourself:
- What is something I do regularly that takes longer than it should?
- Who do I know who struggles with something that a device or system could help with?
- What in my school or neighborhood could work better with a designed solution?

### Lens 2: Problems in Your Engineering Field of Interest

Go back to your Engineering Field Exploration notes from Assignment 1. What kinds of challenges do engineers in that field actually work on? Is there a smaller-scale version of one of those challenges that you could tackle?

For example:
- A student interested in biomedical engineering might consider an assistive device for someone with a physical limitation.
- A student interested in environmental engineering might consider a water quality monitoring system.
- A student interested in electrical/computer engineering might consider a home automation or sensor project.

### Lens 3: Problems You Have Noticed But Never Seen Solved

Sometimes the best engineering projects address a gap that exists not because nobody cares, but because the people who care most don't have the engineering skills to solve it. Look for situations where a technical solution is clearly needed but doesn't exist or isn't accessible.

!!! tip "Generate Before You Filter"
    Write down every idea that comes to mind — aim for at least five to eight. Do not judge or eliminate ideas during the brainstorm. You will narrow the list down afterward. Ideas that seem silly at first sometimes lead to the most interesting projects when you push on them.

## Narrowing to One Topic

Once you have a list of ideas, evaluate each one against the five criteria above. Then ask yourself these three final questions:

1. **Do I understand the problem well enough to explain it to someone who has never seen it?** If not, more research is needed before you can design a solution.
2. **Can I imagine building something for this in school?** Think about the tools, materials, and equipment available to you.
3. **Am I genuinely interested in this?** Engineering projects are harder when you do not care about what you are building.

Choose the one idea that scores best on these questions. This is your project topic.
