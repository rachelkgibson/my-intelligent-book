# Portfolio Development Lab

## Objectives

- Create a professional engineering portfolio organized around your skills and experience.
- Select and present artifacts that demonstrate your technical and problem-solving abilities.
- Develop a portfolio you can share with employers or postsecondary programs.

## What Is an Engineering Portfolio?

A **work portfolio** complements your resume by using visuals to clarify the technical details of your design projects. Where a resume tells an employer you built something, a portfolio *shows* them what you built, how you approached it, and what you learned. Engineers at MIT's Communication Lab describe it this way: a portfolio "presents you as a capable, experienced candidate for the positions you're pursuing."

Two important things to understand upfront:

1. **A portfolio is not a scrapbook.** It is a curated, strategic selection of your strongest work — not everything you have ever done.
2. **Reviewers skim fast.** Employers and admissions officers typically spend 30 seconds to one minute on each project page. Your portfolio needs to communicate clearly at a glance.

### PDF vs. Website

Your portfolio can take two forms. Each has real tradeoffs:

| Format | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **PDF** | Easy to customize per recipient, portable, consistent formatting | Static — no video or interactive content; can't update after sending |
| **Website** | Supports video and GIFs, can update anytime, links easily from a resume | Requires setup time; layout varies by screen |

For this assignment, either format is acceptable. PDF is the easier starting point; a website gives you more flexibility over time. Google Sites, Wix, and Adobe Portfolio are all free options if you go the website route.

## How Many Projects Should You Include?

MIT's CommLab recommends **3–4 projects** for a strong engineering portfolio. That number is deliberate — too few and you look inexperienced, too many and the reader loses focus.

The projects you choose should:

- **Show technical range** — if possible, include projects that demonstrate different skills (design, analysis, fabrication, software, teamwork)
- **Be your best work** — quality over quantity, always
- **Be relevant to your audience** — if you're applying to a mechanical engineering program, lead with your most mechanical project; for a software role, reorder accordingly

!!! tip "Keep a Master Portfolio"
    MIT CommLab recommends maintaining a master portfolio with all of your projects, then selecting 3–4 of the most relevant ones for each specific application. You don't have to rebuild from scratch every time — you just re-curate.

The same principle applies right now: gather everything you have, then choose your strongest pieces for this assignment.

## Structuring Each Project Page

Each project in your portfolio needs its own dedicated page (or section). MIT's CommLab describes the ideal structure as an **inverted pyramid** — the most important information at the top, details below, so a skimming reader gets the key message immediately even if they never scroll down.

Here is the structure for each project page:

| Layer | Content | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Title** | Impactful name that conveys the project's meaning broadly | Grabs attention in one line |
| **Outcome** | What you demonstrated or achieved | Answers "so what?" in one sentence |
| **Motivation** | The project objective and constraints | Gives context for the work |
| **Your contribution** | Specifically what *you* did (especially important for group projects) | Shows individual capability |
| **Technical details** | Methods, tools, calculations, materials | Satisfies engaged readers who want depth |
| **Results** | How well objectives were met; quantified when possible | Proves the work was successful |
| **Skills and tools** | List of software, equipment, or techniques used | Makes skills scannable for recruiters |

!!! warning "Group Projects"
    If a project was done as a team, you **must** clearly state your individual role. Reviewers want to know what *you* contributed — not just what the team produced. Use first-person action verbs: "I designed the mounting bracket" rather than "we designed the mounting system."

## The Role of Visuals

MIT CommLab recommends allocating approximately **70% of each project page to visuals**. That is not decoration — it is the primary communication strategy. A well-chosen image communicates technical detail in seconds that a paragraph of text would take minutes to convey.

**What counts as a strong visual:**

- High-quality, well-lit photographs (no clutter in the background)
- CAD renderings or engineering drawings
- Graphs and data visualizations showing performance
- Annotated diagrams with labels pointing to your specific contributions
- Process photos — work in progress, not just the finished product

**Aim for 2–6 images per project.** Mix system-level views (the whole device or structure) with close-up subsystem views (a specific component or joint). Annotate complex images directly with callouts so the reader doesn't have to guess what they're looking at.

!!! tip "Document as You Go"
    The hardest part of building a portfolio is trying to reconstruct photographs and documentation *after* a project is finished. Get in the habit of photographing your work at every stage — rough sketches, early prototypes, testing setups, and final results. You cannot go back and recreate a photo of the bridge before it broke.

## Portfolio Components Checklist

Beyond your 3–4 project pages, a complete engineering portfolio includes a few additional pieces. Use the checklist below to see where you stand.

| Component | What It Shows | Do You Have It? |
|---|---|---|
| Cover page | Name, field of interest, contact info | ☐ |
| Personal statement | Why engineering, what you're pursuing | ☐ |
| Resume | One-page summary of education, skills, experience | ☐ |
| Project pages (3–4) | Technical work with visuals and outcomes | ☐ |
| Skills and tools list | Software, equipment, lab techniques | ☐ |
| Certifications or awards | External recognition of your work | ☐ |
| Reflection (optional) | What you learned, what you'd do differently | ☐ |

Not every student will have everything on this list right now — that is expected. The goal is to identify what you have, present it as strongly as possible, and note what you want to add over time.

### The Three-Question Test

Before including anything in your portfolio, ask:

1. **Does it demonstrate a skill?** Show something you can *do*, not just something you attended.
2. **Does it show your thinking?** Process evidence (sketches, iterations, test data) is more compelling than a polished final product alone.
3. **Is it your best current work?** Five excellent pieces beat fifteen mediocre ones every time.

## Writing About Your Work

The text on each project page should be brief and punchy — bullet points and short statements, not paragraphs. Use **strong action verbs** to start every bullet:

- *Designed* a load-bearing truss structure using SolidWorks
- *Analyzed* failure points from three bridge prototypes and iterated on the joint geometry
- *Tested* the final design against a 50 lb load requirement and achieved 94% of target
- *Presented* findings to a panel of three engineers at the regional competition

Notice what those bullets have in common: each starts with a verb, names a specific action, and includes a concrete detail (software used, load value, audience). That pattern — verb + action + detail — is the standard for engineering communication at every level.
