# Final Project Presentation

## Objectives

- Deliver a professional presentation of your completed engineering project to stakeholders.
- Communicate your design process, prototype, test results, and reflections clearly and confidently.
- Demonstrate the professional readiness skills you have developed throughout the course.

## Introduction

You have built something. You have tested it, iterated on it, documented it, and written about it. Now you present it.

The Final Project Presentation is your opportunity to communicate — clearly, confidently, and professionally — what problem you solved, how you solved it, what you learned in the process, and what you would do differently given more time or resources. The audience includes people who have not followed your project week by week — they need you to tell the whole story in a way they can evaluate.

The habits you built during the Proposal Pitch (Assignment 14) — eye contact, pacing, handling questions, designing slides — all apply here, at higher stakes.

## Stakeholder Communication

Before finalizing your presentation, identify your audience and tailor your communication to them.

**Stakeholders** are all individuals with an interest in your project's outcome. For a final presentation, they typically include:

- **Technical reviewers** (your teacher, invited engineers) — can evaluate design decisions, test methodology, and data quality
- **Non-technical stakeholders** (parents, community members, administrators) — care about impact, feasibility, and communication quality
- **Peers** (classmates) — evaluate both the engineering work and the communication

A technical stakeholder can handle design specifications and test data. A non-technical stakeholder needs the same information framed in terms of impact and plain language. A strong final presentation bridges both audiences — concrete data for the engineers, human impact framing for everyone else.

## Final Presentation Structure

A well-structured final presentation typically follows this sequence:

| Section | Length | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | 30 sec | Open with the problem in human terms: who is affected, how significantly |
| **Problem Statement** | 1–2 min | Define the challenge, design criteria, and constraints |
| **Design Process** | 2–3 min | Key decisions, iterations, and the prototype — show it, don't just describe it |
| **Results** | 2–3 min | Test data and performance against your design criteria |
| **Reflection** | 1–2 min | What worked, what did not, and what you would do next |
| **Q&A** | 3–5 min | Prepared responses to likely questions; know your data |

**Total: 10–15 minutes** (your teacher will specify the exact time limit)

### Four Qualities of a Strong Final Presentation

1. **Clarity** — The audience understands the project, why it matters, and what you did without needing clarification.
2. **Evidence** — Claims are backed by data, test results, or documented observations — not just assertions.
3. **Narrative** — The presentation tells a coherent story: problem → process → solution → results → reflection. It does not just list what you did.
4. **Professionalism** — Dress, delivery, slide design, and demeanor match the norms of a professional engineering review.

## Professional Readiness

Completing your practicum project is not just an academic achievement — it is evidence of **professional readiness**, the combination of technical skills, communication abilities, work habits, and personal qualities that employers and postsecondary programs look for in early-career engineers.

Professional readiness is demonstrated through actions, not claims. The behaviors you have practiced throughout this course — meeting deadlines, documenting your work, incorporating feedback, communicating with stakeholders, and recovering from setbacks — are exactly what distinguish a prepared professional from someone who merely knows the theory.

Reflect on these dimensions as you prepare your final presentation:

- **Technical competence** — Can you apply engineering tools and methods to a real problem?
- **Project management** — Did you stay on schedule and within budget? How did you recover when you did not?
- **Communication** — Can you explain your work clearly in writing and in person?
- **Ethics and integrity** — Did you document honestly, cite your sources, and prioritize safety?
- **Adaptability** — How did you respond when a test failed, a material was unavailable, or your initial design did not work?

These are also the themes your portfolio should highlight. After the course, update your portfolio with your final technical report, a photo or video of your prototype, a slide from your presentation, and a brief written reflection on what you learned.
