### What Does It Take to Get There?

Every engineering specialization has a defined educational pathway. **Degree requirements** are the specific courses, credit hours, and cumulative GPA thresholds a student must satisfy to earn an engineering degree. While exact requirements vary by school and discipline, most accredited engineering programs share a common structure:

- **Mathematics and basic sciences** — calculus, differential equations, linear algebra, chemistry, and physics
- **Engineering sciences** — discipline-specific foundational courses (circuit analysis, thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, etc.)
- **Engineering design** — a senior capstone project that mirrors real professional work
- **General education** — composition, social sciences, and humanities
- **Technical electives** — specialized courses that allow students to focus on a sub-specialty

**ABET accreditation** (Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology) is the most important quality signal when evaluating **engineering programs**. Employers and licensing boards recognize ABET-accredited programs as meeting national standards. Always check for ABET accreditation before committing to a school.

## Part 4: Professional Certifications and Licensure

!!! success "Skills Assessed"

### A Deeper Dive

Professional credentials in engineering come in two forms: **certifications** and **licenses**. Understanding the difference — and knowing what each requires — is essential for planning your career.

A **professional certification** is a credential awarded by a professional organization that demonstrates competence in a specific area or technology. Certifications are voluntary but valued by employers. Examples include the Project Management Professional (PMP), Certified Energy Manager (CEM), and LEED AP. Requirements vary, but most involve passing an exam and sometimes accumulating work experience.

**Licensure** is the legal authorization — issued by a state government — that permits an engineer to offer professional engineering services to the public. The path to licensure has four stages:

1. **ABET-accredited degree** — Graduate from an accredited engineering program
2. **FE Exam / EIT** — Pass the Fundamentals of Engineering exam to earn the **Engineer in Training (EIT)** designation
3. **Supervised experience** — Work at least four years under a licensed Professional Engineer
4. **PE Exam** — Pass the discipline-specific Principles and Practice of Engineering exam to earn the **Professional Engineer (PE) License**

The PE License is the highest standard credential in the profession. Once licensed, a PE can sign and seal engineering documents and take legal responsibility for designs.
